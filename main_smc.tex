Adaptive Tutoring Beyond Classical RAG:\\

\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is effective for evidence-grounded question answering, but tutoring quality depends on broader system behavior: learner diagnosis, scaffold sequencing, rubric alignment, and calibrated help. We present a repository-grounded adaptive tutoring system that routes each example among classical RAG, hybrid agentic RAG, and coordination-first non-RAG pipelines. The implementation combines a lightweight regime router, specialist-agent orchestration, conditional retrieval with fallback logic, and profile-aware benchmark evaluation. Using frozen metrics from EduBench and TutorEval, we find regime-dependent winners rather than a single dominant architecture. On fully converged TutorEval runs, the hybrid path provides the strongest quality-efficiency frontier (Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, keypoint recall, latency, and token cost). On partial EduBench trajectories, coordination-heavy routes currently lead rubric-oriented metrics and throughput, while retrieval-heavy routes remain stronger on grounding and score-alignment signals. The evidence supports a practical conclusion: in tutoring systems, retrieval is often best treated as a conditional capability inside adaptive orchestration rather than as a fixed top-level controller.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Classical RAG became a default architecture for grounding language-model outputs in external evidence \cite{lewis2020rag}. That default is appropriate for many factual tasks, but tutoring introduces additional control requirements: deciding when to explain directly versus scaffold, modeling learner state, aligning feedback with rubrics, and balancing correctness with pedagogical strategy. Education-focused benchmarks increasingly evaluate these dimensions directly \cite{xu2025edubench,chevalier2024sciencetutors,macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}.

The central question of this paper is not whether multi-agent systems should universally replace retrieval. Instead, we ask \emph{when retrieval-first control is sufficient for educational workloads, and when orchestration-first control becomes justified}. This framing follows recent evidence that coordination policy can dominate performance once tasks require decomposition, critique, and staged execution \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}.

We make three contributions. First, we present an implementation-grounded comparison among executable architecture families in the repository: \texttt{classical\_rag}, \texttt{agentic\_rag}, \texttt{non\_rag\_multi\_agent}, \texttt{single\_agent\_no\_rag}, and \texttt{hybrid\_fast}. Second, we document the methodology at system level: routing logic, conditional retrieval, specialist orchestration, and profile-aware metric computation. Third, we report frozen benchmark evidence with explicit completion-state caveats, separating converged endpoint conclusions (TutorEval) from partial trajectory conclusions (EduBench).

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Recent educational benchmark design shifts beyond short-answer correctness toward pedagogical quality and adaptivity. EduBench evaluates adaptation, pedagogy, and educational utility across multiple educational scenarios \cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval frames science tutoring with open-book and closed-book contexts and keypoint-oriented evaluation \cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. MathTutorBench and TutorBench further emphasize tutoring dialogue quality and rubric-sensitive behavior \cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}.

\textbf{RAG and agentic retrieval.}
Classical retrieval-then-generate pipelines remain a strong baseline in evidence-grounded settings \cite{lewis2020rag}. Agentic retrieval variants, including self-reflection and corrective retrieval, indicate that retrieval quality depends on control policy rather than retrieval alone \cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag}. In long-context educational settings, additional planning and filtering are often needed because context length alone does not guarantee effective grounding \cite{bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

\textbf{Coordination-heavy architectures.}
Multi-agent studies suggest that explicit role specialization can improve complex workflows with decomposition and verification \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}. Educational tutoring tasks naturally map to role-specialized execution (planner, diagnoser, rubric, tutor, critic), motivating architecture selection by task regime rather than a single static controller.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Task framing and route selection}
We frame educational workloads along two axes: evidence-grounding demand and pedagogical-coordination demand. The runtime uses a \texttt{LightweightRegimeRouter} that combines dataset priors and heuristic scores (evidence, coordination, rubric, planning, tutoring) to emit a route decision. The decision includes architecture family and execution flags such as \texttt{require\_retrieval}, \texttt{use\_rubric\_agent}, and \texttt{use\_critic}.

This design keeps routing transparent and inspectable. A route is not a hidden prompt artifact; it is an explicit policy output recorded in traces. In deployment terms, the router acts as a low-overhead control layer that decides whether the system should prioritize retrieval depth, coordination depth, or a hybrid plan.

\subsection{Specialist orchestration and conditional retrieval}
Agentic and hybrid paths execute planner, diagnoser, and rubric specialists in parallel, then pass their artifacts into tutor synthesis. Critic-based revision can be enabled per route. Crucially, retrieval is conditional in the hybrid path: if route-level evidence demand is low, retrieval can be skipped; if evidence demand is high and initial retrieval returns no chunks, fallback retrieval is triggered with a direct-question re-query.

\subsection{Retrieval, reranking, and context packing}
The retrieval stack uses a weighted hybrid score:
\begin{equation}
s(q,d) = \alpha s_{\text{lex}}(q,d) + \beta s_{\text{char}}(q,d) + \gamma s_{\text{latent}}(q,d),
\end{equation}
where lexical and character TF-IDF channels are combined with optional latent projection. Candidate chunks are merged and deduplicated across query variants, reranked with lightweight pair features (base score, lexical overlap, title overlap, phrase and numeric matches, brevity), and packed with MMR-style selection under strict context budget.

\subsection{Profile-aware evaluation}
Adapters map dataset-specific raw fields into a unified \texttt{BenchmarkExample} schema, but metric activation is profile-aware. EduBench activates score-alignment and JSON-compliance checks; TutorEval activates keypoint and chapter-grounding checks. Shared metrics include overlap, rubric coverage, grounding overlap, latency, and token cost.

For overlap, we use token-level F1:
\begin{equation}
F_1 = \frac{2PR}{P+R},\quad
P = \frac{|T_{pred}\cap T_{gold}|}{|T_{pred}|},\quad
R = \frac{|T_{pred}\cap T_{gold}|}{|T_{gold}|}.
\end{equation}

EduBench score alignment is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\text{EduAlign} = \max\left(0,1-\frac{|\hat{s}-s^*|}{100}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{s}$ is predicted score and $s^*$ is consensus-style reference score.

We also report the implementation's complexity proxy:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{ComplexityUnits} ={}& \text{TotalTokens} + 160\,\text{RetrievalQueries} \\
&+ 90\,\text{RetrievedChunks} + 240\,\text{AgentCount} \\
&+ 30\,\text{TraceEvents}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\section{Results}
\subsection{Primary quality-efficiency comparison}
Table~\ref{tab:frozen_quality_summary} summarizes the frozen snapshot. On converged TutorEval, \texttt{hybrid\_fast} is the strongest overall operating point: best Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, and keypoint recall, with lowest latency and token cost. Relative to classical RAG on TutorEval, hybrid improves Token-F1 by 3.3\% and grounded overlap by 5.0\%, while reducing latency by 5.4\% and token usage by 12.2\%.

On partial EduBench, interpretation is intentionally conservative. Coordination-heavy routes (hybrid and non-RAG multi-agent) currently lead rubric coverage and throughput, while classical RAG remains strongest on grounded overlap and score alignment in the current profile. This split matches the expected regime behavior: retrieval-first helps calibration/grounding in evidence-heavy slices; orchestration-first helps rubric-centric quality and efficiency under coordination-heavy demands.

\section{Discussion}
The empirical pattern is not ``more agents always win.'' Instead, the best architecture depends on where task difficulty resides. When evidence grounding is the bottleneck, retrieval-heavy control provides robust calibration. When pedagogical coordination is the bottleneck, specialist orchestration improves rubric-facing quality and efficiency. Hybrid conditional retrieval succeeds because it keeps retrieval available without paying retrieval cost unconditionally.

This regime-dependent interpretation is also aligned with implementation details in the codebase: explicit route scoring, profile-aware metrics, and fallback-triggered retrieval produce behavior that can be audited rather than inferred from prompt anecdotes.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
This study has four important limits. First, EduBench is partial at freeze time, so architecture ranking on EduBench must be interpreted as trajectory evidence rather than endpoint evidence. Second, cross-dataset comparability is limited by profile-gated metric activation: some zeros are structural non-applicability, not model failure. Third, some reported fields include construction-dependent edge behavior (for example, citation-coverage fallback when no retrieval chunks are present, retrieval recall dependence on expected document IDs). Fourth, this paper reports system-output quality and efficiency, not longitudinal learner outcomes; educational impact claims require human tutoring studies and long-horizon classroom protocols.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a repository-grounded study of adaptive tutoring architectures that compares retrieval-first, orchestration-first, and hybrid policies under shared infrastructure. The key conclusion is architectural and practical: no single design dominates all tutoring regimes. Fully converged TutorEval results favor hybrid conditional retrieval for quality-efficiency balance, while partial EduBench trajectories indicate stronger rubric-oriented behavior from coordination-heavy routes and stronger grounding-alignment from retrieval-heavy routes. For educational AI system design, retrieval should be treated as a conditional capability within adaptive orchestration.
