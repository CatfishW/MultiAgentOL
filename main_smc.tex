\title{\LARGE \bf
Adaptive Tutoring Beyond Classical RAG:\\
Conditional Retrieval in a Repository-Grounded Multi-Agent Study
}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}
\maketitle
\thispagestyle{empty}

\begin{abstract}
Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is effective for knowledge-grounded question answering, but tutoring also requires diagnosis, scaffolding, rubric following, and judgment about when not to answer. We study that mismatch with an adaptive multi-agent tutoring framework that uses regime routing and conditional retrieval to switch among classical RAG, hybrid agentic RAG, and coordination-heavy non-RAG tutoring modes. On repository-grounded 30-example snapshots from EduBench and TutorEval, the preferred architecture changes by benchmark regime: coordination-heavy routes improve rubric-oriented metrics on EduBench, while a hybrid agentic route performs best on TutorEval. In tutoring, retrieval often works best as a conditional tool inside a broader coordination policy rather than as the default top-level controller.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is the default way to ground language-model outputs in external evidence \cite{lewis2020rag}. That default makes sense for fact-heavy question answering, but tutoring adds demands RAG does not meet. A tutor does more than retrieve information. It must infer learner state, decide whether to explain or scaffold, align with a rubric, keep the right tone, and sometimes withhold the answer to preserve the learning objective.

Education benchmarks expose the gap. EduBench and TutorEval show the contrast by evaluating pedagogy, adaptivity, and evidence-grounded tutoring rather than answer accuracy \cite{xu2025edubench,chevalier2024sciencetutors}. Related tutoring benchmarks and taxonomies push the same point from other angles, including MathTutorBench, TutorBench, adaptivity evaluation, and safety-focused tutor assessment \cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,maurya2025aitutoreval,borchers2025adaptivity,hazra2026safetutors}. Across these datasets, correctness remains necessary, but pedagogical quality and calibrated support increasingly determine whether a system is useful. Multi-agent studies strengthen the architectural side of that argument by suggesting decomposition can outperform a monolithic prompt once tasks require planning and coordination \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination}.

This paper asks when classical RAG is sufficient for educational tasks and when agentic or multi-agent orchestration is warranted. We evaluate an \emph{adaptive multi-agent tutoring framework} that routes tasks among specialist agents and invokes retrieval only when evidence warrants it. The \texttt{MultiAgent} codebase implements a lightweight regime router, student-state tracking, conditional retrieval with reranking, a fallback retrieval path in the hybrid pipeline, and architecture families spanning classical RAG, agentic RAG, single-agent, and non-RAG tutoring. Across EduBench and TutorEval, the preferred architecture changes by benchmark regime rather than following a single ranking, suggesting that the key question is not whether multi-agent systems replace retrieval, but when educational tasks justify agentic or multi-agent orchestration as a top-level architecture.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Educational evaluation.} Education-focused LLM evaluation has moved beyond short-form answer accuracy toward pedagogical quality, adaptivity, and safety. EduBench spans question answering, grading, emotional support, and personalized learning support, then scores systems on dimensions such as adaptation, reasoning, and pedagogy \cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval, introduced with \emph{Language Models as Science Tutors}, targets long-context science tutoring over textbook chapters and student-style questions \cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. MathTutorBench and TutorBench likewise treat tutoring quality as a first-class target \cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}. EducationQ adds evidence from multi-agent instructional dialogue, reporting that stronger general models do not automatically deliver stronger teaching interactions \cite{shi2025educationq}. Evaluation work sharpens the trend by formalizing pedagogical taxonomies, adaptivity benchmarks, and safety criteria for AI tutors \cite{maurya2025aitutoreval,borchers2025adaptivity,hazra2026safetutors}. Work on math tutoring reaches a similar conclusion from another angle: final-answer accuracy can look strong even when tutoring dialogues contain substantial instructional errors \cite{gupta2025beyondfinalanswers}. Taken together, these benchmarks make a methodological point: educational assistants should be judged not only by answer correctness but also by how well they calibrate help, adapt to learner state, and avoid unsafe or counterproductive pedagogical behavior. Rather than proposing another benchmark, we ask which system architecture best meets those evaluation demands.

\textbf{RAG and agentic RAG.} Classical RAG grounds generation in retrieved evidence \cite{lewis2020rag}. Later work adds self-reflection, retrieval correction, adaptive retrieval, and evaluation in Self-RAG, CRAG, RetrievalQA, RAGChecker, RAGBench, and BERGEN \cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag,zhang2024retrievalqa,ru2024ragchecker,friel2024ragbench,rau2024bergen,chen2023benchmarkingrag}. Most literature optimizes evidence use for knowledge-grounded tasks. TutorEval-style open-book tutoring raises an additional systems issue: the model must use long context effectively while deciding how much help to provide, a combination that standard long-context studies show is non-trivial \cite{liu2024lostmiddle,bai2024longbench2}. Retrieval quality alone does not resolve the tutoring problem; the system must also decide when retrieval is warranted and how retrieved evidence constrains pedagogical behavior. Multi-agent RAG variants such as MA-RAG and recent comparative studies therefore matter less here as retrieval recipes than as evidence that coordination policy can become the main bottleneck once tasks require planning and staged reasoning \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination}.

\textbf{Multi-agent tutoring.} Multi-agent tutoring is attractive because instructional interaction decomposes naturally into diagnosis, planning, feedback, and critique. AgentTutor is a recent example of personalized multi-turn teaching \cite{liu2026agenttutor}. IntelliCode is a 2026 example of agentic tutoring with centralized learner modeling and role-specialized support \cite{jones2026intellicode}. CogEvo-Edu pushes the design further with hierarchical meta-control and reported gains over static RAG in a domain-specific educational setting \cite{wu2025cogevoedu}. More general agent frameworks such as ReAct and AutoGen reinforce the design lesson that explicit roles and actions can outperform a single undifferentiated prompt on complex tasks \cite{yao2023react,wu2024autogen}. Still, most agent papers are not optimized for educational evaluation targets such as scaffold quality, rubric adherence, or calibrated help. Rather than proposing a general-purpose agent framework, we study an orchestration stack, repository-grounded implementations, and benchmark comparisons across classical RAG, agentic RAG, and non-RAG tutoring.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Architecture}
The repository implements five executable families in \texttt{ConferenceEduSystem}: classical RAG, agentic RAG, non-RAG multi-agent, single-agent no-RAG, and hybrid fast. The default path is not ``more agents everywhere,'' but a lightweight router plus specialist execution. Figure~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates the design.

\subsection{Routing and specialist agents}
The \emph{LightweightRegimeRouter} picks an architecture from dataset priors plus evidence, rubric, planning, and tutoring cues. The design avoids a heavy controller-over-controller stack. If coordination demand is high and evidence demand is low, the router leans toward non-RAG tutoring. If both are high, it shifts toward an agentic or hybrid path. The specialist pool includes planner, diagnoser, retriever, rubric, tutor, and critic roles.

The diagnoser uses a \emph{StudentStateTracker} to infer learner level, confusion cues, misconceptions, and explanation style from dialogue and task metadata. Retrieval uses a hybrid sparse index plus a \emph{LightweightReranker}, while the context packer applies MMR compression. In the \texttt{hybrid\_fast} pipeline, the planner, diagnoser, and rubric agents run in parallel. Retrieval runs only when the route requires external evidence, and the hybrid pipeline includes a fallback retrieval pass when the first pass returns no chunks despite an evidence score of at least 0.45. This behavior is implemented in \texttt{orchestration/pipelines.py}.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
The evaluation code separates answer overlap metrics from educational quality metrics. For EduBench, the repository computes \emph{rubric coverage}, \emph{JSON compliance}, and \emph{score alignment} to a mean reference score from consensus-style model predictions. For TutorEval, it emphasizes \emph{token F1}, \emph{rubric coverage}, and \emph{grounded overlap}; TutorEval key points are merged into the rubric and serve as the gold-answer surrogate when no explicit gold answer is available. The evaluation design borrows from retrieval, fact verification, dialogue, and long-context benchmarking traditions rather than treating tutoring as short-form QA \cite{thakur2021beir,thorne2018fever,yang2018hotpotqa,dinan2019wizard,shaham2022scrolls,bai2024longbench2}. General agent benchmarks make the same systems point: model quality and system quality are related but not identical \cite{liu2023agentbench,maseval2026}. We report latency because deployment must balance quality and responsiveness.

\subsection{Repository-grounded results}
Table~\ref{tab:main} reports repository metrics after 30 examples per architecture, keeping sample counts aligned. Some entries are architecture-dependent by construction: grounded overlap is non-zero only for retrieval-based routes, and score alignment appears only for EduBench. Two patterns matter. First, in these snapshots, the multi-agent variants score higher on several tutoring-oriented metrics than classical RAG, but the gains are metric-specific, not universal. On EduBench, multi-agent coordination improves rubric coverage and score alignment, while grounded overlap is non-zero only for the classical RAG route because the metric depends on retrieved chunks. On TutorEval, the hybrid-fast route yields the best token F1, rubric coverage, and grounded overlap in the 30-example snapshot, while reducing latency relative to classical RAG.

Second, the strongest route depends on task demands. EduBench leans toward coordination-heavy tutoring under the repository's rubric-style scoring, so the non-RAG multi-agent path gives the strongest rubric coverage and score alignment in the snapshot. TutorEval rewards evidence-grounded tutoring over long textbook context, and the hybrid-fast route performs best among the runs shown. The snapshots show that the preferred architecture shifts by benchmark regime rather than remaining fixed. This supports adaptive routing over a retrieval-first policy.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
Educational assistance is not always described as retrieval with better phrasing. Classical RAG helps when evidence grounding dominates, but tutoring requires learner diagnosis, rubric following, and calibrated teaching strategies.

Retrieval matters only in the right regime. On TutorEval, the hybrid-fast route improves both answer overlap and grounded overlap over non-RAG tutoring in the aligned 30-example snapshot. On EduBench, coordination-heavy tutoring yields stronger rubric-oriented performance under the repository's scoring setup. The evidence supports a systems rule: use classical RAG when evidence lookup is the bottleneck, and justify agentic or multi-agent orchestration when diagnosis, planning, rubric adherence, or selective retrieval become first-order requirements.

The draft has limits. The repository logs expose aligned early-run snapshots rather than full-test runs, EduBench and TutorEval use different supervision signals, and some metrics vary by construction. A camera-ready version should complete the full benchmark passes, report confidence intervals, and add human judgments of tutoring quality. Even so, the evidence supports only a provisional conclusion: architectural choice should depend on whether the task is constrained mainly by evidence access or by pedagogical coordination.
