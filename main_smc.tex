Adaptive Tutoring Beyond Classical RAG:\\

\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation helps base tutor responses on external evidence, but effective tutoring also depends on learner diagnosis, scaffold sequencing, rubric alignment, and calibrated assistance. This study explores whether retrieval should be the default control for adaptive tutoring or a conditional feature within a larger orchestration system. We develop a multi-agent tutoring system grounded in a repository, comparing retrieval-first, coordination-first, single-agent, and hybrid conditional-retrieval setups under the same freeze-time infrastructure. The system integrates lightweight regime routing, specialist-agent coordination, conditional retrieval with fallback, and profile-aware benchmark evaluation. In fully converged TutorEval runs, the hybrid approach offers the best balance of quality and efficiency: it improves Token-F1 by 3.3\% and grounded overlap by 5.0\% compared to retrieval-first, while reducing mean latency by 5.4\% and token use by 12.2\%. In a partial EduBench snapshot, coordination-heavy paths show higher rubric coverage and lower cost, while retrieval-focused paths demonstrate stronger grounding and score alignment signals. These results indicate that tutoring architecture depends on the regime: retrieval is vital for evidence-sensitive tasks but works best when used selectively within an adaptive orchestration system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A student may ask a tutoring system, ``What does this concept mean?'' In such instances, the system primarily needs to retrieve relevant course content or rubric guidance. Conversely, another student might ask, ``Why is my answer incorrect?'' In this case, an effective tutor must do more than simply surface the correct information. It must determine whether the student requires a direct explanation, a scaffolded hint, a rubric-based correction, or even a withholding of the full answer to facilitate learning. What starts as an information request can quickly evolve into an instructional decision-making challenge.

Motivated by this gap, this paper investigates adaptive tutoring as a regime-dependent architecture-selection problem. We develop a repository-grounded multi-agent tutoring system and evaluate retrieval-first, hybrid conditional-retrieval, single-agent, and coordination-heavy configurations under shared freeze-time controls. The main question is whether retrieval should function as the default controller for tutoring or serve as a conditional capability within a broader orchestration policy.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a widely used way to ground language model outputs in external evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}, and it remains effective for fact-based question answering. In tutoring, however, grounded retrieval is only one part of the interaction. A tutoring system may also need to infer the learner's state, decide how much help to provide, align with pedagogical goals, and determine whether the instructional strategy should take precedence over direct answer delivery. The challenge is therefore not whether retrieval is useful, but when retrieval is sufficient and when additional tutoring control may improve the response.

Recent education-centered benchmarks make this tension more visible. Datasets such as EduBench and TutorEval assess not only correctness but also pedagogy, adaptivity, reasoning, and grounded tutoring behavior~\cite{xu2025edubench,chevalier2024sciencetutors,lmsciencetutorrepo}. Related benchmarks and evaluation studies further emphasize pedagogical quality, adaptivity, and safety rather than answer accuracy alone~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,maurya2025aitutoreval,borchers2025adaptivity,hazra2026safetutors,shi2026kmpbench}. In these settings, correctness remains essential, but it is no longer sufficient. These shifts make the architectural question concrete: if a task is constrained mainly by evidence access, retrieval-first control may be appropriate; if the bottleneck lies in learner diagnosis, planning, rubric following, or targeted pedagogical intervention, then the system needs additional tutoring control beyond retrieval alone.

We use \emph{orchestration} to refer to this additional tutoring control: organizing diagnosis, planning, routing, rubric use, critique, and intervention before or during answer generation. Our framework addresses the architectural question through regime routing and conditional retrieval. Instead of assuming that a single architecture should govern all educational tasks, the system compares retrieval-first, hybrid conditional-retrieval, single-agent, and coordination-heavy tutoring approaches. Retrieval is used when evidence is needed, but it is not treated as the default controller in every situation. The guiding idea is that different tutoring regimes impose different combinations of evidence demand and pedagogical coordination demand, so the preferred architecture may vary across tasks.

We report repository-grounded experiments over frozen benchmark snapshots: a fully converged TutorEval endpoint and partial architecture-specific EduBench processed-success subsets. These experiments are not intended as definitive benchmark-wide rankings. Rather, they provide a shared freeze-time snapshot to examine whether architectural preferences change in response to pedagogical and evidence demands. By comparing four configurations with comparable freeze-time outputs, we evaluate whether retrieval-centered designs are sufficient in evidence-sensitive settings and whether conditional retrieval improves the reported quality-efficiency tradeoff under shared freeze-time controls.


\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Recent benchmarks shift beyond short-answer correctness toward pedagogical quality and adaptivity. EduBench evaluates adaptation, pedagogy, and educational utility across nine scenarios~\cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval frames science tutoring with keypoint-oriented evaluation~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. MathTutorBench and TutorBench further emphasize tutoring dialogue quality and rubric-sensitive behavior~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}.

\textbf{RAG and agentic retrieval.}
Classical retrieval-then-generate pipelines remain strong in evidence-grounded settings~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Agentic variants including self-reflection and corrective retrieval indicate that retrieval quality depends on control policy~\cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag}. In long-context educational settings, additional planning is often needed~\cite{bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

\textbf{Coordination-heavy architectures.}
Multi-agent studies suggest that explicit role specialization can improve complex workflows~\cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}. Educational tutoring naturally maps to role-specialized execution (planner, diagnoser, rubric, tutor, critic), motivating architecture selection by task regime.

Collectively, these threads motivate a repository-based comparison of when retrieval-first, orchestration-first, or hybrid tutoring control is best suited to different educational settings.

\section{Methodology}

The framework operationalizes a design space spanning classical RAG, agentic RAG, non-RAG multi-agent, and conditional-retrieval hybrid paths. Every benchmark example is converted into a unified \texttt{BenchmarkExample} contract and routed to one of five execution families (Figure~\ref{fig:exec_families}). The frozen comparison uses four configurations with comparable outputs; \texttt{agentic\_rag} is implemented but excluded from quantitative claims. The key idea is \emph{controlled adaptivity}: the framework selects the cheapest architecture appropriate for the current task regime.

\subsection{Routing and execution families}

The router receives a benchmark instance together with any dialogue history, rubric, inline context, and modality signals. It estimates lightweight evidence and coordination scores, then returns a route decision containing the predicted task regime, architecture family, and execution flags---whether retrieval is required, whether rubric support is needed, and whether a critic pass should be scheduled.

Figure~\ref{fig:exec_families} summarizes the five execution families. The hybrid fast path is the proposed method because it preserves multi-agent coordination without paying retrieval cost on every example.

\subsection{Specialist agents and shared context}

All execution families operate on a shared \texttt{AgentContext} that carries the original example, route decision, budget policy, any inferred student state, retrieved evidence, planner-produced search queries, rubric summaries, and draft answers for post-hoc revision. This unified contract allows the same tutor and critic modules to be reused across different architecture families without modification.

The planner, diagnoser, and rubric agent are intentionally cheap---their job is not to solve the task in isolation, but to create a structured intermediate state before the tutor drafts the final response. In the hybrid and agentic settings, these specialists run in parallel, which reduces orchestration latency without abandoning role specialization. The \textbf{planner} produces a short execution plan and up to a budgeted set of search queries tailored to the inferred regime. The \textbf{diagnoser} infers learner level, goals, preferred explanation style, and likely misconceptions from dialogue history through lightweight heuristic cues. The \textbf{rubric agent} compresses explicit rubric items into a compact instructional checklist for grading and feedback tasks. The \textbf{tutor} produces the instructional response using the current student profile, planner notes, rubric summary, and evidence. Finally, the \textbf{critic} revises the tutor draft when evidence markers are missing, rubric items are under-covered, or the answer is too long for the learner state.

\subsection{Hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context packing}

The retrieval subsystem is deliberately lightweight. Instead of a heavyweight embedding stack, the repository implements a hybrid index combining word-level TF--IDF, character-level TF--IDF (for typo robustness), and a compact latent projection via truncated SVD. For a query $q$ and candidate chunk $c$, the retrieval score is:
\begin{equation}
s(q,c) = \lambda_w s_w(q,c) + \lambda_c s_c(q,c) + \lambda_\ell s_\ell(q,c) + b(q,c),
\end{equation}
where $s_w$ is lexical similarity, $s_c$ is character-level similarity, $s_\ell$ is similarity in the compact latent space, and $b(q,c)$ is a small query-aware boost for title overlap or citation-sensitive source types.

Retrieved candidates are then passed through a lightweight reranker that reweights the base score using token overlap, title overlap, phrase match, numeric overlap, and brevity. The context packer applies maximum marginal relevance (MMR) so that the selected context remains both relevant and non-redundant, favoring a few complementary snippets over a long list of near-duplicate passages.

The regime router classifies each query and sets execution flags; parallel specialists prepare the shared context; retrieval activates only when the route decision requires evidence; and an optional critic revises the draft.

\subsection{Evaluation}

Metric handling is profile-aware. Shared metrics include token-level F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, latency, and token cost. EduBench additionally tracks score alignment against consensus-style references. Grounded overlap is retrieval-sensitive---it measures how much of the final answer is traceable to retrieved chunks, and is structurally zero when retrieval is disabled.

\section{Experimental Results}

This section presents the experimental design and results for shared prompts, corpora, and budgets, with explicit reporting of subsets. All quantitative claims in this paper are limited to the frozen comparison reported here---a fully converged TutorEval endpoint, partial architecture-specific EduBench processed-success subsets, and four configurations with comparable outputs---and should not be read as benchmark-wide rankings or classroom-effectiveness claims.

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Compared methods.}\quad The frozen comparison uses four configurations (Figure~\ref{fig:exec_families}): Classical RAG (retrieval with minimal coordination), Non-RAG multi-agent (coordination without retrieval), Single-agent (sanity-check baseline), and the proposed \texttt{hybrid\_fast} (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), which uses specialist preparation with conditional retrieval and fallback. The implemented \texttt{agentic\_rag} path is excluded because comparable parity-controlled outputs were not available.


\textbf{Benchmarks.}\quad EduBench spans nine educational scenarios and evaluates responses along twelve teacher- and student-facing aspects~\cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval assesses chapter-grounded science tutoring with keypoint-oriented evaluation~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. Transfer benchmarks (HotpotQA, SCROLLS, LongBench v2, FEVER, Wizard of Wikipedia, BEIR, AgentBench) are retained only for narrower mechanism claims.

\textbf{Parity controls.}\quad All systems share the same indexed corpus, chunking policy, retrieval budget, reranker, packing policy (\emph{retrieval parity}), prompt skeleton, student metadata, and rubric anchors (\emph{prompt parity}). Token counts, tool calls, retrieved chunks, agent count, and latency are logged per run (\emph{budget transparency}). TutorEval is fully converged (834/834); EduBench coverage ranges from 17.9\% to 68.8\%, with substantial variation in success rates across architectures---a signal we later interpret as reflecting system robustness differences (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}). Table~\ref{tab:run_completion_snapshot} reports completion details.

\subsection{Quality-efficiency comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:frozen_quality_summary} summarizes the frozen snapshot. On the fully converged TutorEval endpoint, \texttt{hybrid\_fast} achieves the strongest profile: it obtains the highest Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, and keypoint recall, while also requiring the lowest mean latency and token usage. Relative to classical RAG on TutorEval, hybrid improves Token-F1 by 3.3\% and grounded overlap by 5.0\%, while reducing latency by 5.4\% and token usage by 12.2\%.

For EduBench, we report subset-based trajectory evidence rather than endpoint rankings. Coverage varies from 17.9\% (\texttt{classical\_rag}) to 68.8\% (\texttt{hybrid\_fast} and \texttt{single\_agent\_no\_rag}), and high failure rates in some sessions (due to intermittent API errors) mean that the successfully evaluated subsets may not be identically distributed. EduBench means are therefore computed over each architecture's successfully completed subset and should not be interpreted as fully controlled head-to-head comparisons. Within this subset-based snapshot, coordination-heavy routes (\texttt{hybrid\_fast} and \texttt{non\_rag\_multi\_agent}) show substantially higher rubric coverage (0.972 and 0.977 respectively) and nearly halved latency and token usage compared to retrieval-first and single-agent paths. Classical RAG is the only architecture that achieves non-zero grounded overlap (0.303), confirming that retrieval remains necessary for evidence traceability. EduAlign scores are low across all architectures, with classical RAG showing the strongest signal (0.084); because EduBench uses consensus-style rather than gold-answer supervision, low EduAlign may reflect structural evaluation properties rather than model failure.

The converged TutorEval endpoint and the more substantial EduBench subset evidence together support a conditional design rule: retrieval remains necessary for evidence-sensitive tutoring behavior, but unconditional retrieval-first control is not uniformly optimal in quality-efficiency space. Architecture adaptivity, not family label alone, is the central systems variable in this snapshot.

Four repository-aligned ablations are planned for future analysis: (1)~incorporating the \texttt{agentic\_rag} path to assess whether specialist preparation before retrieval enhances retrieval-intensive tutoring; (2)~disabling conditional retrieval in the hybrid pathway to separate the effects of route-specific retrieval from planner and critic modules; (3)~enabling retrieval for the strongest non-RAG multi-agent baseline to distinguish grounding failures from coordination issues; and (4)~omitting the critic phase to evaluate the impact of post-hoc revision. \section{Discussion}

Our findings challenge the prevailing assumption that retrieval-augmented generation should serve as the default controller for educational AI systems. The results reveal a more nuanced picture: architecture effectiveness is regime-dependent, and no single design dominates across all metrics.

Three key observations emerge. First, retrieval remains indispensable for evidence traceability---only retrieval-enabled architectures achieve non-zero grounded overlap---but unconditional retrieval incurs unnecessary latency and token costs on coordination-heavy tasks. Second, the hybrid conditional-retrieval design achieves the strongest overall quality-efficiency profile on converged TutorEval, suggesting that \emph{selective} evidence grounding outperforms \emph{unconditional} grounding when combined with specialist preparation. Third, the substantial variation in EduBench completion rates across architectures (Table~\ref{tab:run_completion_snapshot}) is itself informative: \texttt{non\_rag\_multi\_agent} exhibits near-perfect reliability (2 failures out of 6,350 processed), while retrieval-dependent paths show higher failure rates under identical API conditions, indicating that retrieval-free coordination is inherently more robust to infrastructure instability.

These observations redirect the design question from ``which family is best?'' to ``how should retrieval, planning, diagnosis, and critique be distributed across task demands?'' In the framework presented here, this distribution is made explicit through regime routing, specialist activation flags, and conditional retrieval gates---yielding an auditable system whose behavior can be traced to architectural decisions rather than prompt engineering.


\section{Limitations}
Although EduBench coverage reaches 68.8\% for some architectures, the evaluation subsets are not identically distributed: high failure rates for \texttt{hybrid\_fast} (7004/11660) and \texttt{single\_agent\_no\_rag} (8774/11670) mean metrics are computed over differently sampled subsets. EduBench results should therefore be interpreted as strong directional evidence rather than fully controlled endpoint comparisons. The snapshot compares complete configurations rather than controlled ablations, \texttt{agentic\_rag} is excluded, and the paper assesses system output quality under benchmarked conditions rather than long-term learner outcomes.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We present a systematic study of architecture selection for adaptive tutoring, comparing retrieval-first, orchestration-first, and hybrid conditional-retrieval designs within a shared evaluation infrastructure. Our central finding is that \emph{no single architecture universally dominates}: on fully converged TutorEval, the proposed \texttt{hybrid\_fast} achieves the best quality-efficiency balance (+3.3\% Token-F1, +5.0\% grounded overlap, $-$5.4\% latency vs.\ classical RAG), while on the larger-scale EduBench evaluation, coordination-heavy approaches consistently demonstrate superior rubric coverage (0.972--0.977 vs.\ 0.747--0.851) with nearly halved latency and token consumption.

Notably, the variation in EduBench completion rates across architectures serves as a proxy for system robustness. Under identical infrastructure and API conditions, \texttt{non\_rag\_multi\_agent} completed 6,348 of 6,350 attempts (99.97\% success), while \texttt{classical\_rag} succeeded on only 2,585 of 3,030 (85.3\%) and \texttt{single\_agent\_no\_rag} on just 2,896 of 11,670 (24.8\%). The hybrid path occupies a middle ground with 4,656 of 11,660 successes (39.9\%), processing the most items overall. This pattern suggests that multi-agent coordination with selective retrieval provides a more fault-tolerant execution profile than either retrieval-only or minimal-agent designs, particularly in production environments subject to API instability.

These results support viewing adaptive tutoring as a \emph{regime-dependent architecture selection problem} rather than a retrieval-first pipeline design. Retrieval remains essential for evidence-sensitive tasks but functions most effectively as a conditional capability within broader orchestration. Future work should extend this study with fully converged EduBench runs, human evaluations of tutoring quality, controlled ablations isolating the contributions of conditional retrieval, specialist preparation, and critic revision, and deployment-aware evaluation under resource-constrained settings.
