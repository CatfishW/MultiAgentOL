\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation grounds tutor responses in external evidence, but effective tutoring also requires learner diagnosis, scaffold sequencing, and rubric alignment. We study adaptive tutoring as an architecture-selection problem: should retrieval be the default controller, or invoked only when the regime demands explicit evidence? We compare retrieval-first, coordination-first, single-agent, and hybrid conditional-retrieval configurations under shared freeze-time controls. On fully converged TutorEval (n=834), hybrid shows numerically higher Token-F1 (+3.3\%), grounded overlap (+5.0\%), and lower latency ($-$5.4\%) and tokens ($-$12.2\%) vs.\ retrieval-first. On partial EduBench with architecture-specific non-overlapping subsets (4.0\%-13.3\% coverage), within-subset patterns suggest coordination-heavy paths achieve higher rubric coverage with lower cost, while retrieval-focused paths retain stronger grounding. Results suggest retrieval is essential for evidence-sensitive tutoring but most effective when used selectively within broader orchestration.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A student may ask a tutoring system, ``What does this concept mean?'' In that case, the system mostly needs to surface the right material. Another student may ask, ``Why is my answer incorrect?'' Now the challenge is different: the tutor must decide whether to explain directly, offer a scaffolded hint, align feedback to a rubric, or deliberately withhold part of the answer to preserve the learning opportunity. What looks like a retrieval problem can quickly become an instructional decision problem.

This paper studies adaptive tutoring through that lens. We frame tutoring as a regime-dependent architecture-selection problem and ask a simple systems question: should retrieval be the default controller, or should it be called only when the task truly demands explicit evidence? To answer it, we build a repository-grounded multi-agent tutoring system and compare retrieval-first, hybrid conditional-retrieval, single-agent, and coordination-heavy configurations under shared freeze-time controls.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is an effective way to ground model outputs in external evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}, and it remains a strong default for fact-based question answering. Tutoring, however, asks for more than grounded recall. A useful tutor may need to infer learner state, choose how much help to provide, follow pedagogical constraints, and decide when instructional strategy should take priority over direct answer delivery. The question, then, is not whether retrieval helps. It is when retrieval is enough and when additional tutoring control matters.

Recent education-centered benchmarks make that distinction harder to ignore. EduBench and TutorEval evaluate not only correctness but also pedagogy, adaptivity, reasoning, and grounded tutoring behavior~\cite{xu2025edubench,chevalier2024sciencetutors,lmsciencetutorrepo}. Related benchmarks and evaluation studies similarly emphasize pedagogical quality, adaptivity, and safety rather than answer accuracy alone~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,maurya2025aitutoreval,borchers2025adaptivity,hazra2026safetutors,shi2026kmpbench}. In this setting, a system can be correct yet still tutor poorly by giving the wrong level of help, missing rubric criteria, or failing to adapt to the learner.

We use \emph{orchestration} to denote the additional control required for those cases: diagnosis, planning, routing, rubric use, critique, and intervention before or during answer generation. Our framework makes this control explicit through regime routing and conditional retrieval. Rather than assuming one architecture should govern every tutoring interaction, we compare retrieval-first, hybrid conditional-retrieval, single-agent, and coordination-heavy approaches under the same infrastructure. Retrieval is available when evidence is needed, but it is not treated as the controller by default.

We report repository-grounded experiments over frozen benchmark snapshots: a fully converged TutorEval endpoint and partial architecture-specific EduBench processed-success subsets. These results are not benchmark-wide rankings. Instead, they offer a controlled snapshot for testing whether architectural preferences shift with evidence demand and pedagogical coordination demand. Within that snapshot, we ask whether retrieval-centered designs are sufficient for evidence-sensitive tutoring and whether conditional retrieval improves the observed quality-efficiency tradeoff under shared freeze-time controls.


\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Recent educational benchmarks move the target beyond answer correctness toward pedagogy, adaptivity, and instructional safety. EduBench spans nine tutoring scenarios and scores educational utility dimensions such as adaptation and pedagogy~\cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval operationalizes chapter-grounded science tutoring with keypoint-based evaluation~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors,lmsciencetutorrepo}. MathTutorBench, TutorBench, and recent evaluation studies further emphasize open-ended pedagogical quality, adaptivity, and safety rather than final-answer accuracy alone~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,maurya2025aitutoreval,borchers2025adaptivity,hazra2026safetutors,shi2026kmpbench}. These benchmarks clarify the gap our paper targets: a tutor can be factually correct yet still fail by giving the wrong level of help, neglecting rubric criteria, or responding unsafely.

\textbf{RAG and agentic retrieval.}
Classical RAG improves evidence grounding by conditioning generation on retrieved external context~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Subsequent work shows that retrieval quality depends strongly on the control policy around it: self-reflective and corrective methods decide when to retrieve again, critique evidence, or revise the answer path~\cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag}. A parallel evaluation literature studies retrieval-heavy systems through answer quality, retrieval diagnostics, and benchmarking infrastructure~\cite{chen2023benchmarkingrag,ru2024ragchecker,friel2024ragbench,rau2024bergen,zhang2024retrievalqa}. Our setting differs in two ways. First, tutoring queries are not always evidence-seeking; some are dominated by diagnosis, hinting, or rubric-following. Second, long educational contexts make naive ``retrieve by default'' behavior brittle, especially when relevant guidance may be diffuse or buried in the prompt context~\cite{bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}. This makes tutoring a natural testbed for \emph{conditional} retrieval rather than unconditional retrieval-first control.

\textbf{Multi-agent tutoring and coordination-heavy control.}
A broad multi-agent literature argues that explicit role specialization can help with planning, critique, and decomposition in complex workflows~\cite{liu2023agentbench,wu2024autogen,li2023camel,hong2024metagpt}. In retrieval settings, collaborative agents have also been proposed to improve search and reasoning over evidence~\cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination}. Closer to our problem, recent tutoring systems such as AgentTutor, IntelliCode, and CogEvo-Edu instantiate specialized educational roles including learner modeling, multi-turn teaching, and collaborative tutoring components~\cite{liu2026agenttutor,jones2026intellicode,wu2025cogevoedu}. These systems motivate tutoring-specific orchestration, but they do not isolate the architectural question we study here: when should evidence retrieval be the primary controller, and when should it remain a subordinate capability inside a broader tutoring policy?

Taken together, prior work gives us strong ingredients---educational benchmarks that reward pedagogy, retrieval methods that improve grounding, and multi-agent systems that improve coordination---but leaves the boundary between them under-specified. Our contribution is a repository-grounded comparison that makes this boundary explicit by testing retrieval-first, coordination-first, and hybrid conditional-retrieval control under shared infrastructure and freeze-time conditions.

\section{Methodology}

The framework spans classical RAG, agentic RAG, non-RAG multi-agent, single-agent no-RAG, and a conditional-retrieval hybrid path. A lightweight router selects execution family and flags (Figure~\ref{fig:exec_families}). The frozen comparison uses four configurations with comparable outputs; \texttt{agentic\_rag} is excluded. The central design is \emph{controlled adaptivity}: use retrieval only when an instance needs external evidence.

\subsection{Routing and execution families}

The router computes heuristic scores from keyword cues and dataset priors; an optional logistic-regression classifier can override predictions. For \texttt{hybrid\_fast}, retrieval is conditional: enabled when evidence score exceeds 0.35. Figure~\ref{fig:exec_families} summarizes the five execution families.

\caption{Repository-grounded execution families. Solid boxes are always-active modules; dashed boxes are optional. The proposed \texttt{hybrid\_fast} path (highlighted) uses conditional retrieval with fallback, combining specialist coordination and selective evidence grounding.}
\label{fig:exec_families}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Specialist agents and shared context}

All execution families operate on a shared \texttt{AgentContext} carrying the example, route decision, student state, search queries, rubric summary, retrieved chunks, and draft answer. Specialists are lightweight and launched in parallel: the \textbf{planner} emits search queries; the \textbf{diagnoser} infers learner level and misconceptions; the \textbf{rubric agent} creates a checklist from rubric items. The \textbf{tutor} drafts the response using retrieved evidence when present. The optional \textbf{critic} checks for concrete issues (missing evidence markers, rubric gaps) and revises when detected.

\subsection{Hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context packing}

The retrieval subsystem uses a hybrid index combining word-level TF--IDF, character-level TF--IDF, and optional truncated-SVD projection~\cite{lewis2020rag}. For query $q$ and chunk $c$: $s(q,c) = \lambda_w s_w(q,c) + \lambda_c s_c(q,c) + \lambda_\ell s_\ell(q,c) + b(q,c)$, where $s_w$, $s_c$, $s_\ell$ are lexical, character, and latent similarities, and $b(q,c)$ boosts title overlap. A lightweight reranker applies weighted scoring (token overlap, phrase match, brevity), then maximum marginal relevance packing selects final chunks.

\subsection{End-to-end hybrid procedure}

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the \texttt{hybrid\_fast} pipeline. The router classifies each example and sets execution flags; parallel specialists (planner, diagnoser, rubric agent) prepare the shared context; retrieval activates only when the route requires evidence; the tutor drafts an answer; and a fallback retrieval attempt is triggered only when no chunks were obtained and the evidence score remains high ($\geq 0.45$). An optional critic revises the draft when concrete issues are detected.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/framework_overview.png}
\caption{Overview of the \texttt{hybrid\_fast} pipeline. Solid lines indicate always-active paths; dashed lines indicate conditionally-activated modules. Three parallel specialists prepare the shared context. The retrieval stack activates only when \texttt{require\_retrieval} is set. \subsection{Evaluation}

Metric handling is profile-aware. Shared metrics include exact match, token-level F1, choice accuracy where applicable, citation coverage, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, adaptivity, retrieval-document recall, latency, and agent count. EduBench additionally tracks JSON-format compliance and score alignment against consensus-style reference scores extracted during dataset adaptation. Grounded overlap is retrieval-sensitive because it is computed from token overlap between the final answer and the retrieved chunks; when no chunks are present, it collapses to zero.

\section{Experimental Results}

This section presents the experimental design and results for shared prompts, corpora, and budgets, with explicit reporting of subsets. All quantitative claims in this paper are limited to the frozen comparison reported here---a fully converged TutorEval endpoint, partial architecture-specific EduBench processed-success subsets, and four configurations with comparable outputs---and should not be read as benchmark-wide rankings or classroom-effectiveness claims.

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Compared methods.}\quad The frozen comparison uses four configurations (Figure~\ref{fig:exec_families}): Classical RAG (retrieval with minimal coordination), Non-RAG multi-agent (coordination without retrieval), Single-agent (sanity-check baseline), and the proposed \texttt{hybrid\_fast} (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), which uses specialist preparation with conditional retrieval and fallback. The implemented \texttt{agentic\_rag} path is excluded because comparable parity-controlled outputs were not available.


\textbf{Benchmarks.}\quad EduBench spans nine educational scenarios and evaluates responses along twelve teacher- and student-facing aspects~\cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval assesses chapter-grounded science tutoring with keypoint-oriented evaluation~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. Transfer benchmarks~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa,shaham2022scrolls,bai2024longbench2,thorne2018fever,dinan2019wizard,thakur2021beir,liu2023agentbench} are retained only for narrower mechanism claims.

\textbf{Parity controls.}\quad All systems share the same indexed corpus, chunking policy, retrieval budget, reranker, packing policy (\emph{retrieval parity}), prompt skeleton, student metadata, and rubric anchors (\emph{prompt parity}). Token counts, tool calls, retrieved chunks, agent count, and latency are logged per run (\emph{budget transparency}). TutorEval is fully converged (834/834); EduBench remains a freeze-time partial trajectory with architecture-specific coverage ranging from 4.0\% to 13.3\%. \subsection{Quality-efficiency comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:frozen_quality_summary} summarizes the frozen snapshot. On the fully converged TutorEval endpoint, \texttt{hybrid\_fast} shows numerically higher Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, and keypoint recall, with lower mean latency and token usage. Relative to classical RAG on TutorEval, hybrid shows +3.3\% Token-F1, +5.0\% grounded overlap, $-$5.4\% latency, and $-$12.2\% token usage.

For EduBench, we report subset-based trajectory evidence. Coverage varies from 4.0\% to 13.3\%, and architectures were evaluated on different, non-overlapping subsets that preclude direct comparison. Within each subset: coordination-heavy routes achieve rubric coverage of 0.954--0.955 with lower latency and tokens; classical RAG is the only architecture with non-zero grounded overlap (0.338), confirming retrieval's role in evidence traceability; EduAlign scores remain low (classical RAG highest at 0.242), likely reflecting consensus-style supervision rather than model failure. Exact Match, Token-F1, and choice accuracy are structurally 0.0 in this profile.

The converged TutorEval endpoint and the more substantial EduBench subset evidence together support a conditional design rule: retrieval remains necessary for evidence-sensitive tutoring behavior, but unconditional retrieval-first control is not uniformly optimal in quality-efficiency space. Architecture adaptivity, not family label alone, is the central systems variable in this snapshot.

The current frozen snapshot evaluates complete architecture configurations rather than isolated ablation variants. Four repository-aligned ablations are planned for future analysis: (1)~incorporating the \texttt{agentic\_rag} path to assess whether specialist preparation before retrieval enhances retrieval-intensive tutoring; (2)~disabling conditional retrieval in the hybrid pathway to separate the effects of route-specific retrieval from planner and critic modules; (3)~enabling retrieval for the strongest non-RAG multi-agent baseline to distinguish grounding failures from coordination issues; and (4)~omitting the critic phase to evaluate the impact of post-hoc revision. These ablations are facilitated by the repository structure but are not employed as quantitative evidence in this snapshot.

\section{Discussion}

Retrieval remains necessary for evidence traceability—only retrieval-enabled architectures achieve non-zero grounded overlap. On fully converged TutorEval, hybrid conditional-retrieval shows numerically higher quality-efficiency scores, suggesting \emph{selective} evidence grounding may outperform \emph{unconditional} grounding when paired with specialist preparation. The partial EduBench snapshot shows within-subset patterns where coordination-heavy paths achieve higher rubric coverage with lower cost, while retrieval-focused paths retain clearer grounding signals. These results shift the design question from ``which family wins?'' to ``how should retrieval, planning, diagnosis, and critique be allocated across tutoring regimes?''

\section{Limitations}
TutorEval is fully converged, but EduBench remains a partial snapshot (13.3\% coverage) with architecture-specific subsets precluding direct comparison. Some metrics have profile-specific semantics: Exact Match, Token-F1, and choice accuracy are structurally zero in this EduBench profile. The snapshot compares complete configurations rather than controlled ablations and evaluates benchmarked outputs rather than downstream learning gains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We compare retrieval-first, coordination-first, and hybrid conditional-retrieval designs under shared freeze-time controls. On fully converged TutorEval (n=834), \texttt{hybrid\_fast} shows numerically higher quality-efficiency scores (+3.3\% Token-F1, +5.0\% grounded overlap, $-$5.4\% latency vs.\ classical RAG). Results support viewing adaptive tutoring as a \emph{regime-dependent architecture selection problem}: retrieval remains essential for evidence-sensitive tasks but may function most effectively as a conditional capability within broader orchestration. Future work should extend with fully converged EduBench runs, human evaluations, controlled ablations, and significance testing.
