\begin{abstract}
Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) assumes a retrieve-then-read controller, but educational AI tutoring often needs context conditioned on learner state and rubric rather than the query alone. This paper explores whether a multi-agent coordination policy can replace that retrieve-first controller while keeping the downstream tutor agent fixed. We compare four retrieval mechanisms under matched infrastructure with a shared open-weight tutor/critic backbone. Our training-free multi-agent retrieval pipeline lets planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules assemble pedagogical context before a regime-aware gate decides whether retrieval is needed. On TutorEval and on the intersection of EduBench, the proposed pipeline improves the shared tutor's quality metrics over classical RAG while reducing token use; on EduBench it also lowers latency and backbone-call count, showing that the gains are not explained by spending more tutor or critic invocations. Replication on a \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8} backbone preserves the efficiency advantage and retains the EduBench quality gains, while TutorEval reaches a quality ceiling for both methods. These results support a narrower but practically important conclusion: in educational tutoring, multi-agent coordination can replace the retrieve-first controller by changing the context supplied to the tutor. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has become the default mechanism for grounding language-model outputs in external evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Its pipeline is fixed by design: the user query is embedded, top-$k$ passages are fetched from an index, and a reader conditions on those passages to generate an answer. This pipeline is optimized for \emph{query-determined} evidence--the retrieved chunks are a function of the surface text of the query. Educational tutoring breaks that assumption. What a tutor should say to a student who asks \emph{``please explain phase to me''} depends not only on which textbook chunk mentions ``phase'' but on the learner's prior level, the rubric the grader will use, and the misconception most likely behind the question. When the retrieval target depends on learner state, embedding the raw query directly is the wrong abstraction.

A natural alternative is to let a small group of specialist agents do the work that classical RAG assumes a single embedded query can do. A planner can propose a scoped search intent; a diagnoser can infer the learner's level and likely misconception; a rubric agent can compile the criteria the answer will be graded against; and only then does a retriever (if anyone) consult the corpus. We refer to this family of systems as \emph{multi-agent retrieval}. Unlike classical RAG, the retriever is no longer the top-level controller; it becomes an optional tool inside a coordination policy that has already built a representation of what the learner needs.

This paper explores whether multi-agent retrieval can replace classical RAG as the grounding mechanism in educational tutoring. We formulate the comparison as a controlled ablation over four retrieval mechanisms that share the same Qwen3.5-4B tutor/critic backbone (reasoning-token budget 512), the same shared corpus, the same reranker, and the same per-call response cap: (i) \textsc{Classical RAG} (retrieve-always, retrieve-then-read), (ii) \textsc{Multi-Agent (no retrieval)}, which replaces the retriever with parallel planner, diagnoser, and rubric specialists, (iii) \textsc{Single-Agent (no retrieval)} as an ablation floor, and (iv) our \textsc{Multi-Agent Retrieval} mechanism (\texttt{hybrid\_fast}), in which the coordination modules run first and a lightweight router then decides per example whether the retriever should be called. The contrast is deliberate: all four mechanisms see the same prompt; the only thing that varies is who--or what--decides when and whether the corpus is consulted. We do \emph{not} force equal total system structure per example; Table~\ref{tab:updated_quality_summary} therefore reports observed backbone-call count as an efficiency outcome rather than assuming it is constant.

Two education benchmarks give us the regime variety we need: they make the source of useful grounding context ambiguous, ranging from external instructional evidence to learner-, rubric-, and scenario-conditioned context. TutorEval~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors} stresses the evidence-grounded regime, where the right passage usually exists inside a textbook chunk. EduBench~\cite{xu2025edubench} spans grading, emotional support, and personalized-learning scenarios where the useful context depends more on learner profile and rubric than on a single chunk. Related educational-evaluation work~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,hazra2026safetutors} reinforces the methodological point: a grounding mechanism cannot be judged on retrieval recall alone. On TutorEval ($n{=}834$ identical examples) and on the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$ identical \texttt{example\_id}s), the same tutor scores higher when fed multi-agent retrieval context while using fewer tokens; on EduBench it also reduces latency and backbone calls.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} (i) We provide a controlled tutoring-side comparison of four retrieval mechanisms under a shared backbone, corpus, reranker, and per-call cap. (ii) We release a training-free pedagogical router and open framework in which only the retrieval-context modules vary: planner, diagnoser, rubric, router, and retriever change, while the tutor and critic are held constant. Released configs pin \textsc{Qwen3.5-4B} and \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8}; scripts reproduce every cell. (iii) On TutorEval ($n{=}834$) and the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), paired bootstrap CIs and permutation tests show that multi-agent retrieval beats classical RAG on fixed-tutor outcome and efficiency metrics while reducing observed backbone invocations on both benchmarks.

\section{Related Work}

Our work sits at the intersection of three lines: classical RAG and its extensions, agentic/multi-agent retrieval, and educational tutoring benchmarks that are sensitive to the grounding mechanism. We survey each line with an eye on one question--\emph{what serves the role classical RAG defines as ``the retriever''?}--because that is the question our experiments answer.

\textbf{Classical RAG and retrieval-control extensions.}
Classical RAG fixes a retrieve-then-read controller: embed the query, retrieve top-$k$ passages, and condition the reader on the retrieved context~\cite{lewis2020rag}. A growing body of work relaxes this controller. Self-RAG trains reflection tokens to decide when to retrieve and critique generations~\cite{asai2024selfrag}; CRAG inserts a retrieval evaluator that triggers corrective actions on low-confidence passages~\cite{yan2024crag}; Adaptive-RAG routes by learned question complexity~\cite{jeong2024adaptiverag}; FRAG estimates query hop complexity and switches retrieval pipelines without extra LLM calls~\cite{gao2025frag}; LLM-Independent Adaptive RAG gates retrieval from external question features rather than model uncertainty~\cite{marina2025llmindependentrag}; RetrievalQA audits when retrieval helps short-form QA~\cite{zhang2024retrievalqa}; and TARG uses a training-free uncertainty gate from a short no-context draft~\cite{wang2026targ}. Selective retrieval is therefore not new. Our claim is narrower: we do not introduce the first adaptive retrieval gate; we test a tutoring-specific gate whose routing signal is a pedagogical regime summary produced \emph{before} retrieval from planner, diagnosis, and rubric context and whose output can dispatch among retrieve-first, retrieval-free, and hybrid families. Evaluation and long-context work further show that strong retrieval alone does not guarantee grounded generation~\cite{chen2023benchmarkingrag,ru2024ragchecker,friel2024ragbench,rau2024bergen,bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

\textbf{Agentic and multi-agent retrieval.}
A parallel line treats retrieval as one tool inside a larger agentic loop. ReAct interleaves reasoning and acting so the model can decide \emph{when} to call a search tool~\cite{yao2023react,wu2024autogen}; AutoGen generalizes this into multi-agent collaboration with role specialization~\cite{wu2024autogen}; CAMEL and MetaGPT push role decomposition further~\cite{li2023camel,hong2024metagpt}. MA-RAG and related coordination studies show that decomposing RAG across multiple agents improves performance on tasks that require planning and staged reasoning~\cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination}, and AgentBench documents how agentic control affects downstream task performance more broadly~\cite{liu2023agentbench}. These systems implicitly argue that a coordination stack can perform the \emph{role} classical RAG assigns to the retriever; we make the argument explicit via a head-to-head comparison under shared infrastructure.

\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Education-focused LLM evaluation has moved beyond short-form answer accuracy toward pedagogy, adaptivity, and instructional safety. EduBench spans nine tutoring scenarios and scores educational utility dimensions such as adaptation and pedagogical application~\cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval targets chapter-grounded science tutoring with keypoint-based evaluation~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. MathTutorBench, TutorBench, and related evaluation frameworks sharpen open-ended pedagogical assessment and tutor-specific safety criteria~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,hazra2026safetutors}. These benchmarks make the methodological point we lean on: when evaluation measures rubric adherence and calibrated help, a system's \emph{grounding mechanism} cannot be judged on retrieval recall alone--because a correct chunk can still lose rubric coverage if the reader does not know the learner. Table~\ref{tab:positioning} summarizes how our multi-agent retrieval gate relates to the RAG-extension line: it is training-free like classical RAG, activates per example like Self-RAG and CRAG, but--unlike those--the \emph{retrieval context} itself is assembled by a tutoring-specific coordination stack (rubric, diagnosis, planner) that runs \emph{before} the retrieval decision, leaving the downstream tutor and critic unchanged.

\textbf{Multi-agent tutoring and positioning.}
Recent tutoring systems instantiate specialized educational roles--AgentTutor~\cite{liu2026agenttutor} and IntelliCode~\cite{jones2026intellicode}--and present themselves as agentic tutors, but from a grounding standpoint they are also candidate replacements for classical RAG: the diagnoser and planner produce the context the reader eventually generates from, even when no explicit retriever is involved. What is missing--and what we contribute--is a head-to-head comparison that treats retrieval, coordination, and their composition as \emph{alternative grounding mechanisms} under shared infrastructure and a frozen backbone.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

All four mechanisms share the same tutor/critic backbone, corpus, reranker, context packer, and response budget; the only designed difference is \emph{how supporting context is assembled before the tutor writes}. Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} sketches the proposed hybrid pipeline. For each benchmark we freeze one common retrieval index and keep its construction unchanged across mechanisms. We omit one additional agentic-retrieval variant from the main table because it does not have a fully matched slice on every benchmark; including it would reintroduce unequal-example-set comparisons. The design principle is simple: retrieve when external evidence is genuinely needed, and otherwise spend the budget on building a better pedagogical view of the learner and the grading criteria. Backbone calls are therefore treated as an observed efficiency outcome rather than a quantity forced to be equal by design.

\subsection{Regime-aware routing}

The router decides which mechanism is most appropriate for each example by estimating two high-level needs: how strongly the answer depends on external evidence, and how strongly it depends on pedagogical coordination such as student diagnosis or rubric interpretation. These judgments are made from the prompt, available context, rubric signals, and benchmark-specific cues. When evidence demand is high and coordination demand is modest, the system follows classical retrieve-then-read behavior. When pedagogical coordination is dominant and explicit evidence need is weak, the system follows the retrieval-free multi-agent path. The remaining cases are handled by the proposed hybrid mechanism, in which the specialist agents first prepare pedagogical context and retrieval is then called only if the example still appears evidence-grounded. If the intermediate draft remains weakly grounded, the router can trigger one fallback retrieval pass. In all reported experiments, this routing policy is fixed before evaluation and applied unchanged across benchmarks.

\subsection{Specialist agents and shared context}

All four retrieval mechanisms operate over the same shared state: the tutoring prompt, the inferred learner profile, the rubric summary, any retrieved evidence, and the evolving draft answer. \textbf{The tutor and the critic are held constant across all four mechanisms}: the tutor writes from the supplied context, and the critic revises only when it identifies a concrete issue. The \emph{only} moving parts are the upstream modules that prepare retrieval context. The \textbf{planner} formulates what information the system should look for, the \textbf{diagnoser} infers learner level and likely misconceptions, the \textbf{rubric agent} summarizes what a strong answer should cover, and the router decides whether those signals are sufficient on their own or whether the corpus should also be consulted. This design isolates the effect of changing the retrieval context while leaving the downstream writer unchanged.

\subsection{Hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context packing}

The retrieval stack is intentionally lightweight. We use sparse retrieval rather than a large dense encoder because the benchmark evidence is often expressed through short textbook phrases, rubric terms, and scenario labels. Candidate passages are gathered with complementary lexical views, reranked for relevance, and then filtered for diversity before being packed into a fixed context budget. In practice, the tutor sees at most four retrieved chunks, which keeps the comparison focused on retrieval quality rather than on simply enlarging the context window.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}

Evaluation is dataset-aware. The composite score is each benchmark's overall summary metric. Token-F1 measures token-level overlap with the benchmark reference, and rubric coverage measures how much of the expected grading rubric appears in the response. Latency, total tokens, and backbone-call count capture inference cost. For EduBench, we additionally report EB12D, the mean over its 12 educational dimensions, and EduAlign, which measures how closely the model's assigned score matches the benchmark reference score. For TutorEval, we report tutor-hit, which measures coverage of the expected teaching key points, together with chapter-grounded tutoring quality. Metrics that do not apply to a benchmark are omitted rather than forced to zero. We also record a composite workload audit for efficiency analysis. A retrieval-agnostic corpus-factuality check is supported when a compatible shared index is available, but the archived main comparison predates that audit, so we do not present it as a headline result here.


\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}

We run two frozen head-to-head comparisons under matched infrastructure (\textsc{Qwen3.5-4B}, reasoning-token budget 512, temperature 0.15, response cap 900 tokens). TutorEval is \emph{complete} on $n{=}834$ identical examples across the four mechanisms; EduBench is evaluated on the \emph{intersection} of $n{=}1562$ identical \texttt{example\_id}s processed by all four mechanisms, so every claim compares point estimates computed on the same test set. Retrieval hyperparameters and budget caps are fixed at the \S\ref{sec:methodology} values.

\subsection{Primary comparison: TutorEval}

Table~\ref{tab:updated_quality_summary} reports matched-example comparisons only. Each delta is computed pairwise on the same examples; the 95\% confidence intervals come from paired bootstrap resampling, and the permutation p-values test whether a difference of that size would be unlikely if the two methods were effectively tied. On TutorEval, changing the retrieval context improves token-level agreement with the reference answer (Token-F1, $+0.004$), coverage of the expected teaching key points (tutor-hit, $+0.019$), and rubric coverage ($+0.026$), while also using 484 fewer tokens on average. Latency trends lower but is not statistically reliable ($p{=}0.21$). Retrieval is activated on every TutorEval example, so this benchmark mainly tests whether coordination still helps when the system continues to consult textbook evidence in every case. Even though the hybrid pipeline instantiates more specialist roles on paper, it uses fewer actual tutor/critic calls per example because planning, diagnosis, and rubric construction are lightweight deterministic steps in the released system. Retrieval-free multi-agent and single-agent baselines lag because the fixed tutor cannot reliably recover chapter-specific key points without the corpus.

\subsection{EduBench intersection and the capacity confound}

On the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), the picture flips more strongly. Multi-agent retrieval improves EB12D, the mean over EduBench's 12 educational dimensions ($+0.031$), as well as rubric coverage ($+0.186$), factual-reasoning accuracy ($+0.055$), and scenario adaptation ($+0.038$), while cutting latency by $5{,}923$\,ms, token use by $1{,}794$, and backbone calls by 0.49 per example. Classical RAG keeps a small edge on EduAlign, which measures agreement between the model's score and the benchmark reference score, and on pedagogical application. Retrieval is skipped on every EduBench example in the frozen run, indicating that these prompts are usually better served by learner- and rubric-centered coordination than by external passage lookup. In effect, EduBench exercises the no-retrieval branch of the policy, so the hybrid behaves like multi-agent coordination plus a lightweight routing decision. That is why it nearly ties the retrieval-free multi-agent variant ($\Delta_{\text{EB12D}}\!=\!+0.001$, $p{=}0.61$) while still beating classical RAG on aggregate quality and efficiency.

\subsection{Ablations: attributing the hybrid's gains}
\label{sec:ablations}

Two executed 4B ablations on a shared TutorEval slice ($n{=}100$) isolate the claimed mechanism. \textbf{(A) Gate off / force retrieval} slightly hurts the fixed-tutor scores (Token-F1 $-0.003$, tutor-hit $-0.015$, rubric $-0.025$) while leaving token use nearly unchanged ($-36$), so the conditional gate is not inert book-keeping even on an evidence-heavy benchmark. \textbf{(B) Critic off} -- with the critic shared across all four mechanisms and therefore outside the scope of our innovation -- improves every reported scalar metric while cutting tokens by $27\%$ ($-891$), so the critic is not the source of the hybrid gain.

\textbf{Backbone replication.} We re-ran all four mechanisms on a pinned \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8} backbone with the same endpoint, corpus, router, and per-call response cap. All eight 27B sessions completed. On TutorEval ($n{=}200$), both classical and hybrid saturate the automatic tutor metrics, so the score deltas are indistinguishable from zero, but hybrid still uses $-992$ tokens per example and is $-11{,}845$\,ms faster (both $p{<}10^{-4}$). On the 27B EduBench intersection ($n{=}87$), the 4B retrieval-context pattern repeats: EB12D $+0.032$ ($p{=}2{\times}10^{-4}$), rubric $+0.210$ ($p{<}10^{-4}$), tokens $-1{,}861$, and latency $-22{,}070$\,ms.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Coordination as a retrieval mechanism.} The evidence supports a narrower claim than ``agents are better tutors'': multi-agent coordination can replace the retriever as the way tutoring context is built. On TutorEval, every 4B fixed-tutor score delta favors multi-agent retrieval at $p{\leq}0.05$; on the EduBench intersection, every reported delta except EduAlign and pedagogical-application favors it at $p{<}10^{-4}$. The planner, diagnoser, and rubric specialists do the work classical RAG assigns to one embedded query, while the tutor and critic remain shared infrastructure.

\textbf{Capacity is asymmetric in structure, not in backbone calls.} Hybrid materializes more specialist roles than classical RAG (mean 6 vs.\ 3 on TutorEval, 5 vs.\ 3 on EduBench), so this is not a matched-structure ablation. The extra planner/diagnoser/rubric roles are deterministic lightweight modules, not extra backbone invocations, so actual tutor/critic calls still fall from 1.84 to 1.56 on TutorEval and from 1.97 to 1.48 on EduBench ($p{<}10^{-4}$ in both cases). The broader workload audit is not uniformly lower: hybrid's composite workload score is higher on TutorEval (6{,}279 vs.\ 5{,}243, $+20\%$) and lower on the EduBench intersection (3{,}906 vs.\ 5{,}289, $-26\%$). Lower backbone-call count therefore weakens the capacity objection on EduBench, but TutorEval remains a coordinated rather than compute-normalized win.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Three constraints. \textbf{(i) Residual structural asymmetry.} The study fixes the tutor/critic backbone, per-call cap, corpus, and retriever, but not the full system structure: hybrid adds lightweight planner/diagnoser/rubric modules and more retrieval-query work even when backbone-call count is lower. The composite workload audit makes this explicit: hybrid is $+20\%$ versus classical RAG on TutorEval but $-26\%$ on the EduBench intersection. \textbf{(ii) Coverage, grounding audit, and human evaluation.} The intersections ($n{=}1562$ at 4B, $n{=}87$ at 27B) cover every example classical RAG finished at snapshot time, but not every example the coordination-heavy mechanisms reached; the archived main comparison also predates the retrieval-agnostic corpus-factuality audit, and we report automatic metrics only. \textbf{(iii) Scope.} Two open-weight backbones, one shared corpus per benchmark, English only, and benchmark outputs rather than learning gains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We compared classical RAG with three coordination-based retrieval alternatives under shared infrastructure on two open-weight backbones. On TutorEval ($n{=}834$), multi-agent retrieval improves Token-F1, tutor-hit, rubric coverage, and token use for the same tutor. On the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), it improves EB12D, rubric coverage, latency, tokens, and backbone calls at $p{<}10^{-4}$. In educational tutoring, multi-agent coordination can replace classical RAG as the retrieval mechanism by changing the context the tutor receives rather than the tutor itself.
