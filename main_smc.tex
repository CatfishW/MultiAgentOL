\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds answers in evidence, but tutoring quality also depends on learner diagnosis, scaffold sequencing, rubric alignment, and calibrated help. We implement and compare retrieval-first, coordination-first, and hybrid conditional-retrieval architectures under shared infrastructure. The system combines a lightweight regime router, specialist-agent orchestration, conditional retrieval with fallback logic, and profile-aware benchmark evaluation. Using frozen EduBench and TutorEval metrics, we find no universal winner: the best architecture depends on regime. On fully converged TutorEval runs, the hybrid path gives the best tradeoff for the emphasized metric set (Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, keypoint recall, latency, and token cost). On partial EduBench trajectories, coordination-heavy routes currently show higher rubric coverage with lower mean latency/token usage, while retrieval-heavy routes remain stronger on grounding and score-alignment signals. For the evaluated benchmarks and freeze state, retrieval appears most effective as a conditional capability inside adaptive orchestration rather than as a fixed top-level controller.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Classical RAG became a default architecture for grounding language-model outputs in external evidence \cite{lewis2020rag}. That default is appropriate for many factual tasks, but tutoring introduces additional control requirements: deciding when to explain directly versus scaffold, modeling learner state, aligning feedback with rubrics, and balancing correctness with pedagogical strategy. Education-focused benchmarks increasingly evaluate these dimensions directly \cite{xu2025edubench,chevalier2024sciencetutors,macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}.

Our central question is not whether multi-agent systems should universally replace retrieval; it is \emph{when retrieval-first control is sufficient for educational workloads and when orchestration-first control is justified}. This framing follows recent evidence that coordination policy can dominate performance once tasks require decomposition, critique, and staged execution \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}.

We make three contributions. First, we present an implementation-grounded comparison among the four architecture families present in the frozen artifacts: \texttt{classical\_rag}, \texttt{hybrid\_fast}, \texttt{non\_rag\_multi\_agent}, and \texttt{single\_agent\_no\_rag}. Second, we document the methodology at system level: routing logic, conditional retrieval, specialist orchestration, and profile-aware metric computation. Third, we report frozen benchmark evidence with explicit completion-state caveats, separating converged endpoint conclusions (TutorEval) from partial trajectory conclusions (EduBench).

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Recent educational benchmark design shifts beyond short-answer correctness toward pedagogical quality and adaptivity. EduBench evaluates adaptation, pedagogy, and educational utility across multiple educational scenarios \cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval frames science tutoring with open-book and closed-book contexts and keypoint-oriented evaluation \cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. MathTutorBench and TutorBench further emphasize tutoring dialogue quality and rubric-sensitive behavior \cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}.

\textbf{RAG and agentic retrieval.}
Classical retrieval-then-generate pipelines remain a strong baseline in evidence-grounded settings \cite{lewis2020rag}. Agentic retrieval variants, including self-reflection and corrective retrieval, indicate that retrieval quality depends on control policy rather than retrieval alone \cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag}. In long-context educational settings, additional planning and filtering are often needed because context length alone does not guarantee effective grounding \cite{bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

\textbf{Coordination-heavy architectures.}
Multi-agent studies suggest that explicit role specialization can improve complex workflows with decomposition and verification \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}. Educational tutoring tasks naturally map to role-specialized execution (planner, diagnoser, rubric, tutor, critic), motivating architecture selection by task regime rather than a single static controller.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Task framing and route selection}
We frame educational workloads along two axes: evidence-grounding demand and pedagogical-coordination demand. The runtime uses a \texttt{LightweightRegimeRouter} that combines dataset priors and heuristic scores (evidence, coordination, rubric, planning, tutoring) to emit a route decision. The decision includes architecture family and execution flags such as \texttt{require\_retrieval}, \texttt{use\_rubric\_agent}, and \texttt{use\_critic}.

This design keeps routing inspectable: each route is a policy output recorded in traces, not a prompt side effect. In deployment, the router decides whether to prioritize retrieval depth, coordination depth, or a hybrid plan.

\subsection{Specialist orchestration and conditional retrieval}
Agentic and hybrid paths execute planner, diagnoser, and rubric specialists in parallel, then pass their artifacts into tutor synthesis. Critic-based revision can be enabled per route. Retrieval is conditional in the hybrid path: low evidence demand skips retrieval, while high evidence demand with zero initial chunks triggers a direct-question fallback re-query.

\subsection{Retrieval, reranking, and context packing}
The retrieval stack uses a weighted hybrid score:
\begin{equation}
s(q,d) = \alpha s_{\text{lex}}(q,d) + \beta s_{\text{char}}(q,d) + \gamma s_{\text{latent}}(q,d),
\end{equation}
where lexical and character TF-IDF channels are combined with optional latent projection. Candidate chunks are merged and deduplicated across query variants, reranked with lightweight pair features (base score, lexical overlap, title overlap, phrase and numeric matches, brevity), and packed with MMR-style selection under strict context budget.

\subsection{Profile-aware evaluation}
Adapters map dataset-specific raw fields into a unified \texttt{BenchmarkExample} schema, but metric activation is profile-aware. EduBench activates score-alignment and JSON-compliance checks; TutorEval activates keypoint and chapter-grounding checks. Shared metrics include overlap, rubric coverage, grounding overlap, latency, and token cost.

For overlap, we use token-level F1 ($P$, $R$ denote precision and recall):
\begin{equation}
F_1 = \frac{2PR}{P+R},\quad
P = \frac{|T_{\text{pred}}\cap T_{\text{gold}}|}{|T_{\text{pred}}|},\quad
R = \frac{|T_{\text{pred}}\cap T_{\text{gold}}|}{|T_{\text{gold}}|}.
\end{equation}

EduBench score alignment is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\text{EduAlign} = \max\left(0,1-\frac{|\hat{s}-s^*|}{100}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{s}$ is predicted score and $s^*$ is consensus-style reference score.

We also report the implementation's complexity proxy:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{ComplexityUnits} &= \text{TotalTokens} + 160\,\text{RetrievalQueries} \\
&\quad+ 90\,\text{RetrievedChunks} + 240\,\text{AgentCount} \\
&\quad+ 30\,\text{TraceEvents}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\section{Results}
\subsection{Primary quality-efficiency comparison}
Table~\ref{tab:frozen_quality_summary} summarizes the frozen snapshot. On converged TutorEval and for the metric set emphasized here (Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, keypoint recall, latency, and total tokens), \texttt{hybrid\_fast} provides the best aggregate tradeoff. Relative to classical RAG on TutorEval, hybrid improves Token-F1 by 3.3\% and grounded overlap by 5.0\%, while reducing latency by 5.4\% and token usage by 12.2\%.

For EduBench, we report trajectory signals rather than endpoint rankings. Coordination-heavy routes (hybrid and non-RAG multi-agent) currently show higher rubric coverage with lower mean latency and token usage in this snapshot, while classical RAG remains stronger on grounded overlap and EduAlign in the current profile. Because EduBench supervision in this freeze is consensus-style rather than direct gold-answer style, some overlap metrics are structurally near zero and should not be used alone to rank architectures. This pattern is consistent with the regime hypothesis, but should be treated as preliminary until EduBench reaches converged coverage and controlled regime-level ablations are reported.

\section{Discussion}
The results do not support a simple ``more agents always win'' rule. In this snapshot, retrieval-heavy control is associated with stronger grounding/alignment signals, while coordination-heavy control is associated with stronger rubric coverage and lower mean latency/token usage. Hybrid conditional retrieval remains competitive because it keeps retrieval available without paying retrieval cost unconditionally.

This regime-dependent reading also follows from implementation details in the codebase: explicit route scoring, profile-aware metrics, and fallback-triggered retrieval make behavior auditable rather than anecdotal.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
This study has four important limits. First, EduBench is partial at freeze time, so architecture ranking on EduBench must be interpreted as trajectory evidence rather than endpoint evidence. Second, cross-dataset comparability is limited by profile-gated metric activation: some zeros are structural non-applicability, not model failure. Third, some reported fields include construction-dependent edge behavior (for example, citation-coverage fallback when no retrieval chunks are present, retrieval recall dependence on expected document IDs). Fourth, this paper reports system-output quality and efficiency, not longitudinal learner outcomes; educational impact claims require human tutoring studies and long-horizon classroom protocols.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a repository-grounded study of adaptive tutoring architectures that compares retrieval-first, orchestration-first, and hybrid policies under shared infrastructure. The key conclusion is architectural and practical: no single design dominates all tutoring regimes. Fully converged TutorEval results favor hybrid conditional retrieval for quality-efficiency balance, while partial EduBench trajectories indicate stronger rubric-oriented behavior from coordination-heavy routes and stronger grounding-alignment from retrieval-heavy routes. For the evaluated benchmarks and freeze state, retrieval should be treated as a conditional capability within adaptive orchestration.
