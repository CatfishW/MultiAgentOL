\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds answers in evidence, but tutoring quality also depends on learner diagnosis, scaffold sequencing, rubric alignment, and calibrated help. We implement and compare retrieval-first, coordination-first, and hybrid conditional-retrieval architectures under shared infrastructure. The system combines a lightweight regime router, specialist-agent orchestration, conditional retrieval with fallback logic, and profile-aware benchmark evaluation. Using frozen EduBench and TutorEval metrics, we find no universal winner: the best architecture depends on regime. On fully converged TutorEval runs, the hybrid path gives the best tradeoff for the emphasized metric set (Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, keypoint recall, latency, and token cost). On partial EduBench trajectories, coordination-heavy routes currently show higher rubric coverage with lower mean latency/token usage, while retrieval-heavy routes remain stronger on grounding and score-alignment signals. For the evaluated benchmarks and freeze state, results are consistent with treating retrieval as a conditional capability inside adaptive orchestration, pending full EduBench convergence and controlled ablations.
\end{abstract}
\section{Introduction}

A student asks a tutoring system, ``Why is my answer wrong?'' At first glance, the system may need to retrieve relevant course content or rubric guidance. But an effective tutor must do more than surface the right information. It must decide whether the student needs a direct explanation, a scaffolded hint, a rubric-based correction, or even a deliberate withholding of the full answer in order to support learning. What begins as an information request quickly becomes an instructional decision-making problem.

Retrieval-augmented generation has become the standard way to ground language model outputs in external evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}, and that default works well for fact-based question answering. In tutoring, however, grounded retrieval is only one part of the interaction. A tutoring system may also need to infer learner state, decide how much help to provide, align with pedagogical goals, and determine whether instructional strategy should take precedence over direct answer delivery. The challenge is therefore not whether retrieval is useful, but when retrieval is sufficient and when additional tutoring control is required.

Recent education-centered benchmarks make this tension more visible. Datasets such as EduBench and TutorEval assess not only correctness but also pedagogy, adaptivity, reasoning, and grounded tutoring behavior~\cite{xu2025edubench,chevalier2024sciencetutors,lmsciencetutorrepo}. Related benchmarks and evaluation studies further emphasize pedagogical quality, adaptivity, and safety rather than answer accuracy alone~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,maurya2025aitutoreval,borchers2025adaptivity,hazra2026safetutors,shi2026kmpbench}. In these settings, correctness remains essential, but it is no longer sufficient. These shifts in evaluation criteria make the architectural question more concrete: if a task is constrained mainly by evidence access, then classical RAG may remain the most appropriate top-level design; if the main bottleneck lies instead in diagnosis, planning, routing, rubric following, or targeted pedagogical intervention, then more explicit orchestration may matter more than retrieval alone.

This paper studies a simple question---when is retrieval needed, and when is orchestration justified?---through an adaptive multi-agent tutoring framework with regime routing and conditional retrieval. Here, orchestration refers to the additional control needed to organize tutoring behavior, including diagnosis, planning, routing, critique, and selective intervention beyond retrieval alone. Instead of assuming that one architecture should govern all educational tasks, the framework routes tasks among different execution modes, including classical RAG, a hybrid agentic path, and coordination-heavy non-RAG tutoring. Retrieval is used when evidence is needed, but it is not treated as the default controller in every situation. The central idea is straightforward: the preferred architecture may vary across educational regimes because different tutoring tasks are shaped by different combinations of evidence requirements and pedagogical coordination demands. This motivation is also consistent with recent multi-agent work suggesting that structured coordination can outperform a single undifferentiated prompting strategy once tasks require decomposition, planning, or staged reasoning~\cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination}.

To ground this framing empirically, we report preliminary repository-grounded experiments on aligned 30-example snapshots from EduBench and TutorEval. These experiments are not intended as definitive benchmark results, but as early tests of the paper's central question: whether architecture preference remains stable across educational settings or instead shifts with task regime. By comparing classical RAG, hybrid agentic, and coordination-heavy tutoring routes under aligned snapshot conditions, the experiments provide an initial way to observe when retrieval-centered designs appear sufficient and when more explicit orchestration becomes justified. Our goal is therefore comparative rather than doctrinal. We do not claim that multi-agent systems universally replace retrieval. Instead, we use these preliminary experiments to examine whether preferred architectures change with differences in pedagogical and evidence demands, and whether retrieval is most effective as a conditional capability within a broader coordination strategy.

The broader significance of this question is both practical and methodological. Within education, it suggests that tutoring systems should not be designed as if better grounding alone guarantees better instructional support. They must also account for diagnosis, scaffolding, rubric alignment, selective retrieval, and the cost of additional coordination. More broadly, although this study is grounded in educational tutoring, the underlying design logic may also help inform architecture selection in other settings where grounding, orchestration, and efficiency must be balanced together. In that sense, the contribution of this paper is not to declare a universal winner, but to show that conditional retrieval and adaptive orchestration can provide a more informative design perspective than retrieval-first assumptions alone.



\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Recent educational benchmark design shifts beyond short-answer correctness toward pedagogical quality and adaptivity. EduBench evaluates adaptation, pedagogy, and educational utility across multiple educational scenarios \cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval frames science tutoring with open-book and closed-book contexts and keypoint-oriented evaluation \cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. MathTutorBench and TutorBench further emphasize tutoring dialogue quality and rubric-sensitive behavior \cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench}.

\textbf{RAG and agentic retrieval.}
Classical retrieval-then-generate pipelines remain a strong baseline in evidence-grounded settings \cite{lewis2020rag}. Agentic retrieval variants, including self-reflection and corrective retrieval, indicate that retrieval quality depends on control policy rather than retrieval alone \cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag}. In long-context educational settings, additional planning and filtering are often needed because context length alone does not guarantee effective grounding \cite{bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

\textbf{Coordination-heavy architectures.}
Multi-agent studies suggest that explicit role specialization can improve complex workflows with decomposition and verification \cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}. Educational tutoring tasks naturally map to role-specialized execution (planner, diagnoser, rubric, tutor, critic), motivating architecture selection by task regime rather than a single static controller.

\textbf{Evidential scope of benchmark families.}
Education-specific benchmarks can support claims about pedagogical behavior under benchmarked instructional conditions, while transferable benchmarks primarily support mechanism-level claims about retrieval depth, long-context handling, and coordination behavior. We therefore treat benchmark category as part of claim discipline: mechanism evidence explains \emph{why} architectures differ, but does not by itself justify broad classroom-impact claims.

Collectively, these research threads motivate the current study, not as an additional benchmark or a broad claim about multi-agent systems, but as a repository-based comparison of when retrieval-first, orchestration-first, or hybrid tutoring control is best suited to different educational settings.

\section{Methodology}

The paper's central contribution is not simply a taxonomy of educational tasks, but a repository-grounded hybrid framework that operationalizes the comparison among classical RAG, agentic RAG, non-RAG multi-agent execution, and a conditional-retrieval hybrid path. In the codebase, every benchmark example is converted into a unified \texttt{BenchmarkExample} contract and then routed to one of five execution families: \texttt{classical\_rag}, \texttt{agentic\_rag}, \texttt{non\_rag\_multi\_agent}, \texttt{single\_agent\_no\_rag}, and \texttt{hybrid\_fast}. The key methodological idea is \emph{controlled adaptivity}: rather than assuming every educational query should always invoke retrieval or always invoke many agents, the framework selects the cheapest architecture that remains appropriate for the current task regime.

\subsection{Routing and execution families}

The router receives a benchmark instance together with any dialogue history, rubric, inline context, and modality signals. It estimates lightweight evidence and coordination scores, then returns a route decision containing the predicted task regime, architecture family, and execution flags---whether retrieval is required, whether rubric support is needed, and whether a critic pass should be scheduled. This makes the framework a hybrid controller rather than a fixed pipeline.

The routing logic is lightweight by design. It combines keyword-aware evidence and coordination cues, optional dataset priors, and regime-specific thresholds to decide whether the example is best treated as evidence-grounded reasoning, rubric-based feedback, adaptive tutoring, or lesson planning. Each route decision is attached to the pipeline response along with trace events, keeping the system inspectable rather than encoding all control in prompt text alone.

\subsection{Specialist agents and shared context}

All execution families operate on a shared \texttt{AgentContext} that carries the original example, route decision, budget policy, any inferred student state, retrieved evidence, planner-produced search queries, rubric summaries, and draft answers for post-hoc revision. This unified contract allows the same tutor and critic modules to be reused across different architecture families without modification.

The planner, diagnoser, and rubric agent are intentionally cheap---their job is not to solve the task in isolation, but to create a structured intermediate state before the tutor drafts the final response. In the hybrid and agentic settings, these specialists run in parallel, which reduces orchestration latency without abandoning role specialization. The \textbf{planner} produces a short execution plan and up to a budgeted set of search queries tailored to the inferred regime. The \textbf{diagnoser} infers learner level, goals, preferred explanation style, and likely misconceptions from dialogue history through lightweight heuristic cues. The \textbf{rubric agent} compresses explicit rubric items into a compact instructional checklist for grading and feedback tasks. The \textbf{tutor} produces the instructional response using the current student profile, planner notes, rubric summary, and evidence. Finally, the \textbf{critic} revises the tutor draft when evidence markers are missing, rubric items are under-covered, or the answer is too long for the learner state.

\subsection{Hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context packing}

The retrieval subsystem is deliberately lightweight. Instead of a heavyweight embedding stack, the repository implements a hybrid index combining word-level TF--IDF, character-level TF--IDF (for typo robustness), and a compact latent projection via truncated SVD. For a query $q$ and candidate chunk $c$, the retrieval score is:
\begin{equation}
s(q,c) = \lambda_w s_w(q,c) + \lambda_c s_c(q,c) + \lambda_\ell s_\ell(q,c) + b(q,c),
\end{equation}
where $s_w$ is lexical similarity, $s_c$ is character-level similarity, $s_\ell$ is similarity in the compact latent space, and $b(q,c)$ is a small query-aware boost for title overlap or citation-sensitive source types.

Retrieved candidates are then passed through a lightweight reranker that reweights the base score using token overlap, title overlap, phrase match, numeric overlap, and brevity. The context packer applies maximum marginal relevance (MMR) so that the selected context remains both relevant and non-redundant, favoring a few complementary snippets over a long list of near-duplicate passages.

\subsection{Profile-aware evaluation}

Dataset adapters map raw fields into the unified \texttt{BenchmarkExample} schema, and metric handling is profile-aware. The evaluator explicitly activates EduBench-specific JSON compliance and score-alignment checks, while TutorEval keypoint and chapter grounding values are read from frozen artifacts. Shared metrics across both benchmarks include token-level F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, latency, and token cost. EduBench additionally tracks score alignment against consensus-style reference scores, and the definition of grounded overlap is retrieval-sensitive---it measures how much of the final answer is traceable to retrieved chunks, which means it is structurally zero whenever retrieval is disabled.

\section{Experimental Protocol}

This section translates the framework into a repository-grounded experimental design. The goal is not to collapse all educational tasks into a single leaderboard, but to compare architecture families under matched prompts, matched corpora, matched budgets, and explicit reporting of which subset of each benchmark was actually scored.

\subsection{Compared methods}

The implementation exposes five runnable configurations, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:method_comparison}. Classical RAG isolates the value of retrieval with minimal coordination. Agentic RAG tests whether retrieval improves when preceded by lightweight planning and diagnosis. Non-RAG multi-agent isolates coordination without a default retrieval controller. Single-agent no-RAG acts as a minimal sanity anchor. The proposed method is \texttt{hybrid\_fast}, which uses specialist preparation with conditional retrieval and fallback retrieval. The comparison is therefore not only multi-agent versus RAG, but \emph{when} conditional retrieval is preferable to always-on retrieval or to coordination without retrieval.

\subsection{Benchmarks and task fit}

The benchmark shortlist is chosen to separate evidence-sensitive educational tasks from coordination-sensitive ones. EduBench is especially useful because the public benchmark spans nine educational scenarios and evaluates responses along twelve teacher- and student-facing aspects, making it appropriate for architecture comparisons that go beyond answer accuracy alone~\cite{xu2025edubench}. TutorEval remains valuable for chapter-grounded science tutoring, especially for contrasting open-book tutoring behavior with more explanation-centric strategies~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. Transfer benchmarks such as HotpotQA, SCROLLS, LongBench v2, FEVER, Wizard of Wikipedia, BEIR, and AgentBench are retained only for narrower mechanism claims about retrieval depth, grounding, long-context handling, or workflow coordination.

\subsection{Subset-based reporting}

A key reporting decision in this paper is to avoid describing incomplete runs in terms of benchmark coverage percentages alone. In the local repository export, the EduBench evaluation pool contains 16,956 items. When a run completes only part of that pool, we report the exact scored subset rather than a coverage percentage, because a phrase such as ``13\% coverage'' mixes throughput with evaluation quality and invites the wrong conclusion that the percentage itself is an outcome metric. Cross-method results are reported on matched subsets so that architecture comparisons reflect the same examples rather than different partially completed slices.

\subsection{Snapshot protocol and parity controls}

All numbers in this paper come from frozen metrics artifacts generated from repository evaluation runs (freeze timestamp: 2026-04-12 21:18:44 EDT). We evaluate five architecture families across EduBench and TutorEval under the following parity controls. First, \emph{retrieval parity}: whenever retrieval is available, every retrieval-enabled system uses the same indexed corpus, chunking policy, retrieval budget, reranker, and packing policy. Second, \emph{prompt parity}: all systems receive the same prompt skeleton, student metadata, and rubric anchors, with only architecture-specific control flow changing. Third, \emph{budget transparency}: token counts, tool calls, retrieved chunks, agent count, and latency are logged for every run so that quality gains are not confused with simply spending more compute.

To avoid overclaiming, we separate endpoint evidence from trajectory evidence: TutorEval is fully converged (834/834 for all architectures), whereas EduBench is partial (coverage 4.02\% to 13.31\% at freeze). Accordingly, TutorEval supports endpoint comparisons, while EduBench supports trajectory comparisons only. The practical workflow is: run architecture sessions under fixed configuration, freeze run summaries with processed/pending/failed fields, derive consolidated metrics from the frozen summaries, and render manuscript tables from the frozen layer only. This prevents accidental drift between manuscript claims and evolving run directories.

\subsection{Ablation design}

Three ablations are especially important for interpreting the results. First, disabling conditional retrieval in the hybrid path tests whether the benefit comes from the route decision or simply from having planner and critic modules. Second, adding retrieval to the strongest non-RAG multi-agent baseline tests whether its failures are mainly grounding failures or coordination failures. Third, removing the critic pass measures whether performance gains come from better first-pass control or from post-hoc revision. These ablations map directly to the repository structure and therefore support stronger methodological claims than a purely conceptual comparison.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Primary quality-efficiency comparison}
Table~\ref{tab:frozen_quality_summary} summarizes the frozen snapshot. On converged TutorEval, \texttt{hybrid\_fast} is Pareto-favorable on Token-F1, grounded overlap, rubric coverage, keypoint recall, latency, and total tokens, while keypoint precision and chapter grounding favor other architectures in the frozen inventory. Relative to classical RAG on TutorEval, hybrid improves Token-F1 by 3.3\% and grounded overlap by 5.0\%, while reducing latency by 5.4\% and token usage by 12.2\%.

For EduBench, we report trajectory signals rather than endpoint rankings. Coordination-heavy routes (hybrid and non-RAG multi-agent) currently show higher rubric coverage with lower mean latency and token usage in this snapshot, while classical RAG remains stronger on grounded overlap and EduAlign in the current profile. Because EduBench supervision in this freeze is consensus-style rather than direct gold-answer style, some overlap metrics are structurally near zero and should not be used alone to rank architectures. Because EduBench coverage and failure rates differ by architecture at freeze time, and summaries are computed over processed-success subsets, these deltas should be interpreted as directional trajectory signals rather than controlled endpoint effects.

The converged TutorEval endpoint and partial EduBench trajectory together support a conditional design rule: retrieval remains necessary for evidence-sensitive tutoring behavior, but unconditional retrieval-first control is not uniformly optimal in quality-efficiency space. Architecture adaptivity, not family label alone, is the central systems variable in this snapshot.

\section{Discussion}

The current snapshot does not support a straightforward "more agents always win" rule. Instead, it indicates that the effectiveness of retrieval and orchestration depends on the regime. In the frozen results, retrieval-intensive control continues to show stronger grounding and alignment signals, while coordination-focused control exhibits more rubric-oriented behavior and, in some instances, reduced average latency and token consumption. Hybrid conditional retrieval is particularly competitive, as it maintains access to external evidence without incurring unconditional retrieval costs.

This interpretation is critical because it redirects the design focus from family labels to control policy. The key issue is not whether a system is inherently retrieval-based or multi-agent, but how it distributes functions such as retrieval, planning, diagnosis, critique, and tutoring across various task demands. In the current repository, this distribution is explicitly represented through route scoring, specialist activation, profile-aware evaluation, and fallback-triggered retrieval. Consequently, the system's behavior is auditable at an operational level rather than solely attributable to prompt phrasing or anecdotal instances.

The results also reinforce the paper's broader argument that tutoring should not be viewed as a repository-based question-answering task. Certain benchmark conditions favor evidence-sensitive grounding more strongly, while others emphasize rubric alignment, pedagogical structure, and targeted assistance. Under these varying conditions, the optimal architecture may differ across regimes rather than remain fixed. The key takeaway from this study is that selecting an architecture for tutoring should be regarded as a regime-dependent system choice, rather than a competition to identify a universally optimal design.


\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
This study has several significant limitations. First, EduBench is only partially completed at the time of freezing, so the EduBench results should be considered as trajectory evidence rather than definitive endpoint rankings. Second, comparability across datasets remains limited because some metrics are profile-gated; certain zeros or missing values indicate structural non-applicability rather than model failure. Third, several reported fields depend on specific construction details within the current evaluation stack, such as fallback behavior when no retrieval chunks are available and retrieval-sensitive definitions of grounding-related metrics. While these details are important for reproducibility, they also constrain the ability to compare some metrics directly across benchmarks and routes.

An additional limitation concerns the scope of inference. The paper assesses system output quality and efficiency under benchmarked conditions, not longitudinal learner outcomes. Consequently, the current evidence supports architectural and system-level claims regarding tutoring behavior, trade-offs, and regime sensitivity, but cannot by itself justify stronger claims about long-term instructional effectiveness or classroom impact. Addressing these questions requires human evaluation and extended educational studies.


\section{Conclusion}
We conducted a repository-based study on adaptive tutoring architectures, comparing retrieval-first, orchestration-first, and hybrid policies within a shared infrastructure. The primary finding is architectural rather than doctrinal: no single design outperforms all tutoring regimes. In fully converged TutorEval experiments, hybrid conditional retrieval offered the best balance between quality and efficiency for the specified metrics. Under partial EduBench trajectories, coordination-intensive approaches exhibited stronger rubric adherence, whereas retrieval-heavy strategies excelled in grounding and score-alignment signals.

Overall, the results support viewing tutoring systems as a comparison of architectures. While retrieval remains a key component, it is not always the optimal default controller; in the evaluated fixed state, it functions most effectively as a conditional capability within adaptive orchestration. Broadly, these findings suggest that architecture selection should be driven by the specific regime's needs—balancing evidence demand, pedagogical coordination, and efficiency—rather than adhering strictly to a retrieval-first or multi-agent control paradigm.l.

\section{Future Work}
Future work should expand upon this frozen-snapshot study in three key areas. First, the empirical comparison needs to be completed using fully converged benchmark runs, particularly for EduBench, with confidence intervals and more rigorous controls over completion states to enable endpoint evidence to replace trajectory evidence. Second, the evaluation should go beyond current artifact metrics to incorporate human assessments of tutoring quality, more detailed failure-mode analysis, and clearer reporting of cost and latency tradeoffs under shared comparison settings. Third, the architecture question should be examined more systematically through controlled ablations, such as comparisons of the same backbone versus best-fit configurations, in-depth analysis of routing policies, and deployment-aware evaluation in resource-constrained environments. These enhancements will help determine not only which architecture performs better in specific regimes but also the underlying reasons and the robustness of these reasons under realistic tutoring scenarios.
