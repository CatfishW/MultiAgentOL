\begin{abstract}
Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) assumes a retrieve-then-read controller, but educational tutoring often depends on personalized learning state and grading criteria rather than the surface query alone. This paper asks whether a multi-agent coordination policy can replace that retrieve-first controller while keeping the downstream tutor fixed. We compare four retrieval mechanisms under matched infrastructure with a shared open-weight tutor/critic backbone. In the released system, routing is computed from raw sample fields, and the hybrid pipeline then uses planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules to assemble tutoring context before optional retrieval. On TutorEval and on the matched-sample intersection of EduBench, the proposed pipeline improves the fixed tutor's quality metrics over classical RAG while using fewer tokens; on EduBench it also reduces latency and backbone-call count, showing that the gains are not explained by spending more backbone LLM invocations. Replication on a \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8} backbone preserves the efficiency advantage and retains the EduBench quality gains, while TutorEval approaches a quality ceiling for both methods. The result is a narrower but practical conclusion: in educational tutoring, changing how context is assembled before generation can outperform retrieve-first control.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has become the default mechanism for grounding language-model outputs in external evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Its control pattern is fixed by design: the user query is embedded, top-$k$ passages are fetched from an index, and a reader conditions on those passages to generate an answer. This is a strong fit for factual question answering, where the needed evidence is largely determined by the surface query. Educational tutoring is different. In this paper, a \emph{tutor} is the module that produces the final educational response: it may explain a concept, diagnose a misunderstanding, score a student answer, or give supportive next-step feedback. Those tutoring decisions often depend on \emph{personalized learning state}: the student-specific information that should condition the response, such as current level, goals, preferred explanation style, weak points, recent confusion, and likely misconceptions.

This dependence on personalized learning state is visible in both benchmarks we study. TutorEval~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors} is chapter-grounded science tutoring, but many rows are not simple lookup problems. Sample questions such as \emph{``Can you explain how we calculate the heat in Step 4 of Example 1?''} or \emph{``So if I vary the y-intercept of a line, is that like translating the line horizontally?''} require the system to identify the student's confusion and instructional goal before answering. EduBench~\cite{xu2025edubench} makes this even more explicit. Its Personalized Learning Support (PLS) and Personalized Content Creation (PCC) rows include structured or semi-structured student profiles with fields such as grade, proficiency, interests, learning style, study habits, goals, and weak points. Two students can ask for help on the same subject yet require different tutoring actions because their personalized learning state differs.

This is precisely where classical query-only RAG is a weak fit. A retrieve-then-read controller can fetch topic-similar passages from the query, but it has no explicit stage for constructing personalized learning state before retrieval. In EduBench, attributes such as \emph{visual learner}, \emph{beginner reader}, \emph{pronunciation difficulty}, or \emph{studies best in short sessions} are not topic facts that naturally emerge from corpus retrieval. In TutorEval, the bottleneck is often not finding a chapter about the topic, but identifying which misconception, prerequisite gap, or misleading phrasing the tutor should address. Such state can be manually appended to a retrieval query, but then the system is no longer classical retrieve-then-read; it has already introduced task-specific coordination before retrieval.

A natural alternative is therefore to let a small group of specialist modules build a tutoring brief before answer generation. A planner can propose a scoped search intent; a diagnoser can summarize personalized learning state from the prompt and dialogue; a rubric agent can compile what a strong answer should cover; and only then does a retriever (if anyone) consult the corpus. We refer to this family of systems as \emph{multi-agent retrieval}. Unlike classical RAG, the retriever is no longer the top-level controller; it becomes an optional tool inside a coordination policy that has already identified what the learner needs.

This paper explores whether multi-agent retrieval can replace classical RAG as the grounding mechanism in educational tutoring. We formulate the comparison as a controlled ablation over four retrieval mechanisms that share the same Qwen3.5-4B tutor/critic backbone (reasoning-token budget 512), the same shared corpus, the same reranker, and the same per-call response cap: (i) \textsc{Classical RAG} (retrieve-always, retrieve-then-read), (ii) \textsc{Multi-Agent (no retrieval)}, which replaces the retriever with parallel planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules, (iii) \textsc{Single-Agent (no retrieval)} as an ablation floor, and (iv) our \textsc{Multi-Agent Retrieval} mechanism (\texttt{hybrid\_fast}), in which planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules prepare context and retrieval is invoked only when the route indicates it is needed. The contrast is deliberate: all four mechanisms consume the same raw tutoring sample, but they assemble different upstream context before the shared tutor writes. In the benchmark scripts, each architecture is run as a separately forced variant rather than as one live four-way router choosing among them online. We do \emph{not} force equal total system structure per sample; Table~\ref{tab:updated_quality_summary} therefore reports observed backbone-call count as an efficiency outcome rather than assuming it is constant.

Educational tutoring is broader than textbook question answering. A deployed tutor may need to explain a concept, diagnose a misconception from a dialogue turn, score a response against a rubric, recommend a learning path, or produce supportive next-step feedback. Those subproblems require different kinds of grounding: some depend primarily on external instructional evidence, while others depend more on personalized learning state, rubric structure, or scenario framing. Although the released registry treats both TutorEval and EduBench as adaptive-tutoring families, they provide sharply different task structure and supervision. TutorEval is chapter-grounded diagnostic tutoring: each sample pairs a student-style question with a chapter, pedagogical metadata, and expected teaching key points. EduBench is broader and more practice-oriented: its student-facing tasks include question answering, error correction, idea provision, personalized learning support, and emotional support, while its teacher-facing tasks include grading, teaching-material generation, and differentiated content creation. Related educational-evaluation work~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,hazra2026safetutors} reinforces the methodological point: a grounding mechanism cannot be judged on retrieval recall alone. On TutorEval ($n{=}834$ matched samples) and on the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$ matched samples), the same tutor scores higher when fed multi-agent retrieval context while using fewer tokens; on EduBench it also reduces latency and backbone calls.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} (i) We provide a controlled tutoring-side comparison of four retrieval mechanisms under a shared backbone, corpus, reranker, and per-call cap. (ii) We release a training-free pedagogical router and open framework in which only the retrieval-context modules vary: planner, diagnoser, rubric, router, and retriever change, while the tutor and critic are held constant. Released configs pin \textsc{Qwen3.5-4B} and \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8}; scripts reproduce every cell. (iii) On TutorEval ($n{=}834$) and the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), paired bootstrap CIs and permutation tests show that multi-agent retrieval beats classical RAG on fixed-tutor outcome and efficiency metrics while reducing observed backbone invocations on both benchmarks.

\section{Related Work}

Our work sits at the intersection of three lines: classical RAG and its extensions, agentic/multi-agent retrieval, and educational tutoring benchmarks that are sensitive to the grounding mechanism. We survey each line with an eye on one question--\emph{what serves the role classical RAG defines as ``the retriever''?}--because that is the question our experiments answer.

\textbf{Classical RAG and retrieval-control extensions.}
Classical RAG fixes a retrieve-then-read controller: embed the query, retrieve top-$k$ passages, and condition the reader on the retrieved context~\cite{lewis2020rag}. A growing body of work relaxes this controller. Self-RAG trains reflection tokens to decide when to retrieve and critique generations~\cite{asai2024selfrag}; CRAG inserts a retrieval evaluator that triggers corrective actions on low-confidence passages~\cite{yan2024crag}; Adaptive-RAG routes by learned question complexity~\cite{jeong2024adaptiverag}; FRAG estimates query hop complexity and switches retrieval pipelines without extra LLM calls~\cite{gao2025frag}; LLM-Independent Adaptive RAG gates retrieval from external question features rather than model uncertainty~\cite{marina2025llmindependentrag}; RetrievalQA audits when retrieval helps short-form QA~\cite{zhang2024retrievalqa}; and TARG uses a training-free uncertainty gate from a short no-context draft~\cite{wang2026targ}. Selective retrieval is therefore not new. Our claim is narrower: we do not introduce the first adaptive retrieval gate; we test a tutoring-specific route-and-context design in which raw tutoring samples are routed first and the hybrid pipeline then assembles pedagogical context from planning, diagnosis, and rubric signals before optional retrieval. Evaluation and long-context work further show that strong retrieval alone does not guarantee grounded generation~\cite{chen2023benchmarkingrag,ru2024ragchecker,friel2024ragbench,rau2024bergen,bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

\textbf{Agentic and multi-agent retrieval.}
A parallel line treats retrieval as one tool inside a larger agentic loop. ReAct interleaves reasoning and acting so the model can decide \emph{when} to call a search tool~\cite{yao2023react,wu2024autogen}; AutoGen generalizes this into multi-agent collaboration with role specialization~\cite{wu2024autogen}; CAMEL and MetaGPT push role decomposition further~\cite{li2023camel,hong2024metagpt}. MA-RAG and related coordination studies show that decomposing RAG across multiple agents improves performance on tasks that require planning and staged reasoning~\cite{nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination}, and AgentBench documents how agentic control affects downstream task performance more broadly~\cite{liu2023agentbench}. These systems implicitly argue that a coordination stack can perform the \emph{role} classical RAG assigns to the retriever; we make the argument explicit via a head-to-head comparison under shared infrastructure.

\textbf{Educational tutoring benchmarks.}
Education-focused LLM evaluation has moved beyond short-form answer accuracy toward pedagogy, adaptivity, and instructional safety. TutorEval targets chapter-grounded science tutoring with keypoint-based evaluation: each sample is effectively a tutoring response problem in which the model should explain the content, cover the expected teaching points, and stay anchored to the chapter context when needed~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. EduBench is broader and more heterogeneous: it spans grading, feedback, emotional support, and personalized-learning scenarios, and its released supervision is closer to a consensus educational-judgment profile than to a single gold answer~\cite{xu2025edubench}. In our codebase, that distinction appears explicitly as two evaluation profiles: TutorEval rows are scored against key-point coverage, while EduBench rows are scored through structured score, detail, and feedback outputs that feed a 12-dimension educational rubric. MathTutorBench, TutorBench, and related evaluation frameworks sharpen open-ended pedagogical assessment and tutor-specific safety criteria~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,hazra2026safetutors}. These benchmarks make the methodological point we lean on: when evaluation measures rubric adherence and calibrated help, a system's \emph{grounding mechanism} cannot be judged on retrieval recall alone--because a correct chunk can still lose rubric coverage if the reader does not know the learner. Table~\ref{tab:positioning} summarizes how our multi-agent retrieval gate relates to the RAG-extension line: it is training-free like classical RAG, activates per sample like Self-RAG and CRAG, but--unlike those--the hybrid pipeline uses a tutoring-specific coordination stack (rubric, diagnosis, planner) to prepare retrieval context after routing and before the shared tutor writes.

\textbf{Multi-agent tutoring and positioning.}
Recent tutoring systems instantiate specialized educational roles--AgentTutor~\cite{liu2026agenttutor} and IntelliCode~\cite{jones2026intellicode}--and present themselves as agentic tutors, but from a grounding standpoint they are also candidate replacements for classical RAG: the diagnoser and planner produce the context the reader eventually generates from, even when no explicit retriever is involved. What is missing--and what we contribute--is a head-to-head comparison that treats retrieval, coordination, and their composition as \emph{alternative grounding mechanisms} under shared infrastructure and a frozen backbone.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Our proposed framework, \textsc{Multi-Agent Retrieval}, is a training-free tutoring pipeline that combines route-first control with context-first tutoring. We represent each tutoring sample as a bundle of student request, optional dialogue history, optional chapter/context text, rubric information, and benchmark metadata. The released app first computes a route from those raw fields. In the hybrid pipeline, that precomputed route is then used while planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules build a short tutoring brief: what the student is asking, what personalized learning state is visible, what a good answer should satisfy, and whether outside evidence is still needed. The key idea is to move answer generation away from retrieve-first control while keeping the downstream tutor and critic fixed. Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} sketches this pipeline.

This separation matters because the two benchmarks stress different failure modes. In TutorEval, the missing information is often chapter-grounded but pedagogically scoped: the tutor must connect the question to the right example, equation, or misconception inside a long chapter. In EduBench, the missing information is often student-specific: the system must react to profile fields, grading criteria, or scenario constraints that are already present in the sample but are not naturally retrievable from a topical query. A retrieve-first controller treats these cases too similarly. Our pipeline makes the tutoring brief explicit before answer generation, even though the top-level route is computed first.

To isolate that design choice, all implemented mechanisms share the same tutor/critic backbone, corpus, reranker, context packer, and response budget; the only designed difference is \emph{how supporting context is assembled before the tutor writes}. For each benchmark we freeze one common retrieval index and keep its construction unchanged across mechanisms. We omit one additional agentic-retrieval variant from the main table because it does not have a fully matched slice on every benchmark; including it would reintroduce unequal-example-set comparisons. The design principle is simple: retrieve when external evidence is genuinely needed, and otherwise spend the budget on building a better pedagogical view of the learner and the grading criteria. Backbone calls are therefore treated as an observed efficiency outcome rather than a quantity forced to be equal by design. The head-to-head tables should thus be read as controlled pipeline ablations under shared routing metadata, not as a benchmark of one online router making a four-way architecture choice.

\subsection{Regime-aware routing}

The released router is evaluated before any tutoring pipeline runs. It supports three decision sources: a trained bag-of-ngrams classifier when one is loaded, dataset-family priors otherwise, and the cue-based fallback described below. For each sample $x$, the fallback constructs a normalized text blob $b(x)$ from the question, optional context, rubric text, and dialogue history, and computes cue-match rates
\begin{equation}
\kappa_G(x)=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{u\in G}\mathbb{1}[u \subseteq b(x)],
\end{equation}
for five cue sets: evidence ($E$), coordination ($C$), rubric ($R$), planning ($P$), and tutoring ($T$). Intuitively, each score is the fraction of cue phrases from a cue bank that appear in the tutoring sample, with small bonuses when the sample already exposes a context field, rubric, image, or benchmark prior. The released code then constructs
\begin{align}
s_e(x) &= \operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\kappa_E(x) + 0.08\,\mathbb{1}[\text{context}] + 0.05\,\mathbb{1}[\text{image}] + \pi_e(d_x)\right), \\
s_c(x) &= \operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\kappa_C(x) + 0.03\,\mathbb{1}[\text{image}] + \pi_c(d_x)\right), \\
s_r(x) &= \operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\kappa_R(x) + 0.20\,\mathbb{1}[\text{rubric}] + \pi_r(d_x)\right), \\
s_p(x) &= \operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\kappa_P(x) + \pi_p(d_x)\right), \\
s_t(x) &= \operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\kappa_T(x) + \min(|h_x|/6,\,0.5) + \pi_t(d_x)\right),
\end{align}
where $d_x$ is the dataset identity and $h_x$ is the dialogue history. In the paper configs, dataset priors add $+0.15$ to evidence for evidence-grounded datasets, $+0.15$ to coordination and rubric for rubric-feedback datasets, $+0.15$ to coordination and tutoring for adaptive-tutoring datasets, and $(+0.12,+0.18)$ to coordination and planning for lesson-planning datasets. High $s_e$ means the sample looks like an evidence-grounded question; high $s_c$, $s_r$, $s_p$, or $s_t$ mean the sample increasingly looks like a coordination-heavy tutoring, rubric, planning, or dialogue-sensitive case. For TutorEval and EduBench specifically, the released adapters also attach adaptive-tutoring regime hints, so the route combines cue scores with benchmark priors rather than relying on the fallback alone.

Architecture selection is then a fixed case split:
\begin{equation}
A(x)=
\begin{cases}
\textsc{Classical RAG}, & s_e(x)\ge 0.52 \;\land\; s_c(x)<0.35,\\
\textsc{Agentic RAG}, & s_e(x)\ge 0.35 \;\land\; s_c(x)\ge 0.50,\\
\textsc{Multi-Agent (no retrieval)}, & s_c(x)\ge 0.55 \;\land\; s_e(x)<0.28,\\
\textsc{Multi-Agent Retrieval}, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
This case split describes the fallback router's family choices. In words, high evidence with low coordination is handled as classical retrieve-then-read, high evidence with high coordination is handled as agentic RAG, and high coordination with little evidence is handled as multi-agent tutoring without retrieval. Borderline cases fall into the hybrid family. The paper's \textsc{Single-Agent (no retrieval)} baseline is a separate ablation floor and is not produced by the router. The pedagogical regime is resolved separately. If the dataset adapter provides a regime hint, the router uses it directly; otherwise it sets
\begin{equation}
R(x)=
\begin{cases}
\text{lesson planning}, & s_p(x)\ge \max\{s_r(x), s_t(x), 0.42\},\\
\text{rubric feedback}, & s_r(x)\ge \max\{s_t(x), 0.35\},\\
\text{adaptive tutoring}, & s_t(x)\ge 0.35,\\
\text{evidence grounded}, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Within the proposed hybrid family, retrieval is gated by
\begin{equation}
g(x)=\mathbb{1}[s_e(x)\ge 0.35 \;\lor\; R(x)=\text{evidence grounded}],
\end{equation}
which matches the production threshold in the codebase. Inside the hybrid pipeline, if $g(x)=0$, the system still builds the tutoring brief and skips retrieval unless a later fallback is triggered. If the first draft still has no retrieved grounding and $s_e(x)\ge 0.45$, the pipeline issues one fallback retrieval pass using the raw question. This fallback is important on mixed cases where the initial coordination view looks sufficient but the final answer still benefits from chapter or reference evidence.

\subsection{Specialist agents and shared context}

All implemented mechanisms operate over the same shared state: the tutoring prompt, the inferred personalized learning state, the rubric summary, any retrieved evidence, and the evolving draft answer. \textbf{The tutor and the critic are held constant across all implemented mechanisms}: the tutor writes from the supplied context, and the critic revises only when it identifies a concrete issue. The \emph{only} moving parts are the upstream modules that prepare retrieval context. In the released paper configuration, the planner, diagnoser, and rubric agent are lightweight deterministic heuristics rather than separate LLM workers. The planner emits up to three deduplicated search queries formed from the raw question, extracted key terms, dataset cueing, and rubric terms. The diagnoser summarizes personalized learning state as level, goals, preferred style, and likely misconceptions from prompt and dialogue cues. The rubric agent turns explicit rubric items into a short checklist or falls back to a default tutoring rubric.

The tutor itself is dataset-aware but architecturally fixed. Its prompt includes the dataset name, question, recent dialogue, inferred personalized learning state, operating plan, rubric summary, and either retrieved evidence or inline benchmark context. It is always instructed to be correct, pedagogically useful, concise, and to end with a clear next step or check-for-understanding when appropriate. When retrieved evidence is present, the tutor must cite it with \texttt{[doc\_id]} markers. For EduBench consensus rows, the tutor is further constrained to emit a single JSON object containing a score, scoring details, and personalized feedback, because that benchmark evaluates structured educational judgment. For TutorEval and the other tutoring-style profiles, the tutor emits ordinary instructional prose. This design isolates the effect of changing the retrieval context while leaving the downstream writer unchanged.

\subsection{Hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context packing}

The retrieval stack is intentionally lightweight and also follows the codebase exactly. For a query $q$ and chunk $c$, the hybrid index computes
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{score}_0(q,c)=0.58\,k_w(q,c)+0.17\,k_{ch}(q,c)+0.25\,k_{\ell}(q,c)+\beta(q,c),
\end{equation}
where $k_w$ is word-level TF--IDF similarity, $k_{ch}$ is character $3$--$5$ gram TF--IDF similarity, and $k_{\ell}$ is the similarity in the truncated-SVD latent space. The query boost $\beta(q,c)$ adds $0.01$ per overlapping title token and, in citation-seeking mode, an additional $0.025$ for chunks whose source type is \emph{reference}, \emph{lecture}, or \emph{document}. When multiple planner queries are issued, the retriever merges all returned candidates and deduplicates them by chunk identifier before reranking.

In the released paper configuration, no trained reranker is loaded, so the heuristic reranker score is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{score}_1(q,c)=0.55\,\mathrm{score}_0+0.20\,o_{\text{tok}}+0.10\,o_{\text{title}}+0.06\,m_{\text{phrase}}+0.05\,o_{\text{num}}+0.04\,\rho(c),
\end{equation}
where the additional features are token overlap, title overlap, exact phrase match, numeric overlap, and a brevity prior. Final context packing uses maximum marginal relevance:
\begin{equation}
c^\star=\arg\max_{c\in \mathcal{C}\setminus S}
\lambda\,\mathrm{score}_1(q,c)-(1-\lambda)\max_{c'\in S}\mathrm{overlap}(c,c'),
\end{equation}
with $\lambda=0.68$, a four-chunk cap, and a $6000$-character context budget. This keeps the comparison focused on retrieval quality rather than on simply enlarging the context window.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}

Evaluation is dataset-aware, but the reported metrics are internal automatic proxies implemented in the released evaluator rather than official benchmark scoring scripts. The headline table's composite score (\textit{Comp.}) is a paper-level weighted summary from the released analysis pipeline, reported only as a convenience overview; we interpret the task-specific metrics directly. Token-F1 is the token-overlap F1 between the model answer and the benchmark reference answer. Rubric coverage is the fraction of rubric items the answer covers, using either near-verbatim phrase match or sufficiently strong token overlap anchored by a content-bearing token. Latency, total tokens, and backbone-call count capture inference cost, and backbone calls correspond to the released system's \texttt{llm\_call\_count}.

For EduBench, we additionally report EB12D, the mean over 12 rubric-oriented signals spanning scenario adaptation, factual reasoning, and pedagogical application. Concretely, those signals reward output-format compliance, supportive but non-negative tone, relevance to the question, use of scenario elements from the sample, alignment with the benchmark score, domain-knowledge grounding, reasoning quality, error-identification ability, clarity, motivational guidance, personalization, and higher-order prompting. We also report EduAlign,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{EduAlign}(y,\bar{s}) = 1 - \frac{|s(y)-\bar{s}|}{100},
\end{equation}
where $s(y)$ is the score extracted from the model's JSON output and $\bar{s}$ is the benchmark's reference score.

For TutorEval, we report tutor-hit, a soft key-point coverage score,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{TutorHit}(y)=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} h_k(y),
\end{equation}
where $h_k(y)$ gives full credit when key point $k$ is explicitly present in answer $y$, partial credit for moderate token overlap, and zero otherwise. We also track a chapter-grounding proxy by measuring whether the response stays aligned with retrieved evidence when retrieval is used, or with the inline chapter text otherwise. Concretely, EduBench is evaluated as a structured educational-judgment task, whereas TutorEval is evaluated as a chapter-grounded tutoring-response task. In the per-example evaluator outputs, off-profile metrics remain present as zeros; in the paper tables, benchmark-inapplicable columns are simply not shown. The released code also records a composite workload audit
\begin{equation}
U = T + 160Q + 90C + 240A + 30E,
\end{equation}
where $T$ is total tokens, $Q$ retrieval queries, $C$ retrieved chunks, $A$ materialized agent outputs, and $E$ trace events. This score is used only as an efficiency audit, not as a training objective. A retrieval-agnostic corpus-factuality check is supported when a compatible shared index is available, but the archived main comparison predates that audit, so we do not present it as a headline result here.


\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}

We run two frozen head-to-head comparisons under matched infrastructure (\textsc{Qwen3.5-4B}, reasoning-token budget 512, temperature 0.15, response cap 900 tokens). TutorEval is \emph{complete} on $n{=}834$ matched samples across the four mechanisms; EduBench is evaluated on the \emph{intersection} of $n{=}1562$ matched samples processed by all four mechanisms, so every claim compares point estimates computed on the same test set. Retrieval hyperparameters and budget caps are fixed at the \S\ref{sec:methodology} values.

\subsection{Primary comparison: TutorEval}

Table~\ref{tab:updated_quality_summary} reports matched-sample comparisons only. Each delta is computed pairwise on the same samples; the 95\% confidence intervals come from paired bootstrap resampling, and the permutation p-values test whether a difference of that size would be unlikely if the two methods were effectively tied. On TutorEval, changing the retrieval context improves token-level agreement with the reference answer (Token-F1, $+0.004$), coverage of the expected teaching key points (tutor-hit, $+0.019$), and rubric coverage ($+0.026$), while also using 484 fewer tokens on average. Latency trends lower but is not statistically reliable ($p{=}0.21$). Retrieval is activated on every TutorEval sample, so this benchmark mainly tests whether coordination still helps when the system continues to consult textbook evidence in every case. Even though the hybrid pipeline instantiates more specialist roles on paper, it uses fewer actual tutor/critic calls per sample because planning, diagnosis, and rubric construction are lightweight deterministic steps in the released system. Retrieval-free multi-agent and single-agent baselines lag because the fixed tutor cannot reliably recover chapter-specific key points without the corpus.

\subsection{EduBench intersection and the capacity confound}

On the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), the picture flips more strongly. Multi-agent retrieval improves EB12D, the mean over EduBench's 12 educational dimensions ($+0.031$), as well as rubric coverage ($+0.186$), factual-reasoning accuracy ($+0.055$), and scenario adaptation ($+0.038$), while cutting latency by $5{,}923$\,ms, token use by $1{,}794$, and backbone calls by 0.49 per sample. Classical RAG keeps a small edge on EduAlign, which measures agreement between the model's score and the benchmark reference score, and on pedagogical application. Retrieval is skipped on every EduBench sample in the frozen run, indicating that these prompts are usually better served by learner- and rubric-centered coordination than by external passage lookup. In effect, EduBench exercises the no-retrieval branch of the policy, so the hybrid behaves like multi-agent coordination plus a lightweight routing decision. That is why it nearly ties the retrieval-free multi-agent variant ($\Delta_{\text{EB12D}}\!=\!+0.001$, $p{=}0.61$) while still beating classical RAG on aggregate quality and efficiency.

\subsection{Ablations: attributing the hybrid's gains}
\label{sec:ablations}

Two executed 4B ablations on a shared TutorEval slice ($n{=}100$) isolate the claimed mechanism. \textbf{(A) Gate off / force retrieval} slightly hurts the fixed-tutor scores (Token-F1 $-0.003$, tutor-hit $-0.015$, rubric $-0.025$) while leaving token use nearly unchanged ($-36$), so the conditional gate is not inert book-keeping even on an evidence-heavy benchmark. \textbf{(B) Critic off} -- with the critic shared across all four mechanisms and therefore outside the scope of our innovation -- improves every reported scalar metric while cutting tokens by $27\%$ ($-891$), so the critic is not the source of the hybrid gain.

\textbf{Backbone replication.} We re-ran all four mechanisms on a pinned \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8} backbone with the same endpoint, corpus, router, and per-call response cap. All eight 27B sessions completed. On TutorEval ($n{=}200$), both classical and hybrid saturate the automatic tutor metrics, so the score deltas are indistinguishable from zero, but hybrid still uses $-992$ tokens per sample and is $-11{,}845$\,ms faster (both $p{<}10^{-4}$). On the 27B EduBench intersection ($n{=}87$), the 4B retrieval-context pattern repeats: EB12D $+0.032$ ($p{=}2{\times}10^{-4}$), rubric $+0.210$ ($p{<}10^{-4}$), tokens $-1{,}861$, and latency $-22{,}070$\,ms.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Coordination as a retrieval mechanism.} The evidence supports a narrower claim than ``agents are better tutors'': multi-agent coordination can replace the retriever as the way tutoring context is built. On TutorEval, every 4B fixed-tutor score delta favors multi-agent retrieval at $p{\leq}0.05$; on the EduBench intersection, every reported delta except EduAlign and pedagogical-application favors it at $p{<}10^{-4}$. The planner, diagnoser, and rubric specialists do the work classical RAG assigns to one embedded query, while the tutor and critic remain shared infrastructure.

\textbf{Capacity is asymmetric in structure, not in backbone calls.} Hybrid materializes more specialist roles than classical RAG (mean 6 vs.\ 3 on TutorEval, 5 vs.\ 3 on EduBench), so this is not a matched-structure ablation. The extra planner/diagnoser/rubric roles are deterministic lightweight modules, not extra backbone invocations, so actual tutor/critic calls still fall from 1.84 to 1.56 on TutorEval and from 1.97 to 1.48 on EduBench ($p{<}10^{-4}$ in both cases). The broader workload audit is not uniformly lower: hybrid's composite workload score is higher on TutorEval (6{,}279 vs.\ 5{,}243, $+20\%$) and lower on the EduBench intersection (3{,}906 vs.\ 5{,}289, $-26\%$). Lower backbone-call count therefore weakens the capacity objection on EduBench, but TutorEval remains a coordinated rather than compute-normalized win.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Three constraints. \textbf{(i) Residual structural asymmetry.} The study fixes the tutor/critic backbone, per-call cap, corpus, and retriever, but not the full system structure: hybrid adds lightweight planner/diagnoser/rubric modules and more retrieval-query work even when backbone-call count is lower. The composite workload audit makes this explicit: hybrid is $+20\%$ versus classical RAG on TutorEval but $-26\%$ on the EduBench intersection. \textbf{(ii) Coverage, grounding audit, and human evaluation.} The intersections ($n{=}1562$ at 4B, $n{=}87$ at 27B) cover every example classical RAG finished at snapshot time, but not every example the coordination-heavy mechanisms reached; the archived main comparison also predates the retrieval-agnostic corpus-factuality audit, and we report automatic metrics only. \textbf{(iii) Scope.} Two open-weight backbones, one shared corpus per benchmark, English only, and benchmark outputs rather than learning gains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We compared classical RAG with three coordination-based retrieval alternatives under shared infrastructure on two open-weight backbones. On TutorEval ($n{=}834$), multi-agent retrieval improves Token-F1, tutor-hit, rubric coverage, and token use for the same tutor. On the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), it improves EB12D, rubric coverage, latency, tokens, and backbone calls at $p{<}10^{-4}$. In educational tutoring, multi-agent coordination can replace classical RAG as the retrieval mechanism by changing the context the tutor receives rather than the tutor itself.
