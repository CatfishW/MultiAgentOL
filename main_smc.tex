\begin{abstract}
Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) assumes a retrieve-then-read controller, but educational tutoring often depends on personalized learning state and grading criteria rather than the surface query alone. This paper asks whether a multi-agent coordination policy can replace that retrieve-first controller while keeping the downstream tutor fixed. We compare four retrieval mechanisms under matched infrastructure with a shared open-weight tutor/critic backbone. In the released system, routing is computed from raw sample fields, and the hybrid pipeline then uses planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules to assemble tutoring context before optional retrieval. On TutorEval and on the matched-sample intersection of EduBench, the proposed pipeline improves the fixed tutor's quality metrics over classical RAG while using fewer tokens; on EduBench it also reduces latency and backbone-call count, showing that the gains are not explained by spending more backbone LLM invocations. Replication on a \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8} backbone preserves the efficiency advantage and retains the EduBench quality gains, while TutorEval approaches a quality ceiling for both methods. The result is a narrower but practical conclusion: in educational tutoring, changing how context is assembled before generation can outperform retrieve-first control.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has become the default mechanism for grounding language-model outputs in external evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Its control pattern is fixed by design: the user query is embedded, top-$k$ passages are fetched from an index, and a reader conditions on those passages to generate an answer. This is a strong fit for factual question answering, where the needed evidence is largely determined by the surface query. Educational tutoring is different. In this paper, a \emph{tutor} is the module that produces the final educational response: it may explain a concept, diagnose a misunderstanding, score a student answer, or give supportive next-step feedback. Those tutoring decisions often depend on \emph{personalized learning state}: the student-specific information that should condition the response, such as current level, goals, preferred explanation style, weak points, recent confusion, and likely misconceptions.

This dependence on personalized learning state is visible in both benchmarks we study. TutorEval~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors} is chapter-grounded science tutoring, but many rows are not simple lookup problems. Sample questions such as \emph{``Can you explain how we calculate the heat in Step 4 of Example 1?''} or \emph{``So if I vary the y-intercept of a line, is that like translating the line horizontally?''} require the system to identify the student's confusion and instructional goal before answering. EduBench~\cite{xu2025edubench} makes this even more explicit. Its Personalized Learning Support (PLS) and Personalized Content Creation (PCC) rows include structured or semi-structured student profiles with fields such as grade, proficiency, interests, learning style, study habits, goals, and weak points. Two students can ask for help on the same subject yet require different tutoring actions because their personalized learning state differs.

This is precisely where classical query-only RAG is a weak fit. A retrieve-then-read controller can fetch topic-similar passages from the query, but it has no explicit stage for constructing personalized learning state before retrieval. In EduBench, attributes such as \emph{visual learner}, \emph{beginner reader}, \emph{pronunciation difficulty}, or \emph{studies best in short sessions} are not topic facts that naturally emerge from corpus retrieval. In TutorEval, the bottleneck is often not finding a chapter about the topic, but identifying which misconception, prerequisite gap, or misleading phrasing the tutor should address. Such state can be manually appended to a retrieval query, but then the system is no longer classical retrieve-then-read; it has already introduced task-specific coordination before retrieval.

A natural alternative is therefore to let a small group of specialist modules build a tutoring brief before answer generation. A planner can propose a scoped search intent; a diagnoser can summarize personalized learning state from the prompt and dialogue; a rubric agent can compile what a strong answer should cover; and only then does a retriever (if anyone) consult the corpus. We refer to this family of systems as \emph{multi-agent retrieval}. Unlike classical RAG, the retriever is no longer the top-level controller; it becomes an optional tool inside a coordination policy that has already identified what the learner needs.

This paper explores whether multi-agent retrieval can replace classical RAG as the grounding mechanism in educational tutoring. We formulate the comparison as a controlled ablation over four retrieval mechanisms that share the same Qwen3.5-4B tutor/critic backbone (reasoning-token budget 512), the same shared corpus, the same reranker, and the same per-call response cap: (i) \textsc{Classical RAG} (retrieve-always, retrieve-then-read), (ii) \textsc{Multi-Agent (no retrieval)}, which replaces the retriever with parallel planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules, (iii) \textsc{Single-Agent (no retrieval)} as an ablation floor, and (iv) our \textsc{Multi-Agent Retrieval} mechanism (\texttt{hybrid\_fast}), in which planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules prepare context and retrieval is invoked only when the route indicates it is needed. The contrast is deliberate: all four mechanisms consume the same raw tutoring sample, but they assemble different upstream context before the shared tutor writes. In the benchmark scripts, each architecture is run as a separately forced variant rather than as one live four-way router choosing among them online. Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism_comparison} summarizes that controlled comparison. We do \emph{not} force equal total system structure per sample; Table~\ref{tab:updated_quality_summary} therefore reports observed backbone-call count as an efficiency outcome rather than assuming it is constant.

Educational tutoring is broader than textbook question answering. A deployed tutor may need to explain a concept, diagnose a misconception from a dialogue turn, score a response against a rubric, recommend a learning path, or produce supportive next-step feedback. Those subproblems require different kinds of grounding: some depend primarily on external instructional evidence, while others depend more on personalized learning state, rubric structure, or scenario framing. Although the released registry treats both TutorEval and EduBench as adaptive-tutoring families, they provide sharply different task structure and supervision. TutorEval is chapter-grounded diagnostic tutoring: each sample pairs a student-style question with a chapter, pedagogical metadata, and expected teaching key points. EduBench is broader and more practice-oriented: its student-facing tasks include question answering, error correction, idea provision, personalized learning support, and emotional support, while its teacher-facing tasks include grading, teaching-material generation, and differentiated content creation. Related educational-evaluation work~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,hazra2026safetutors} reinforces the methodological point: a grounding mechanism cannot be judged on retrieval recall alone. On TutorEval ($n{=}834$ matched samples) and on the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$ matched samples), the same tutor scores higher when fed multi-agent retrieval context while using fewer tokens; on EduBench it also reduces latency and backbone calls.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} (i) We provide a controlled tutoring-side comparison of four retrieval mechanisms under a shared backbone, corpus, reranker, and per-call cap. (ii) We release a training-free pedagogical router and open framework in which only the retrieval-context modules vary: planner, diagnoser, rubric, router, and retriever change, while the tutor and critic are held constant. Released configs pin \textsc{Qwen3.5-4B} and \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8}; scripts reproduce every cell. (iii) On TutorEval ($n{=}834$) and the EduBench intersection ($n{=}1562$), paired bootstrap CIs and permutation tests show that multi-agent retrieval beats classical RAG on fixed-tutor outcome and efficiency metrics while reducing observed backbone invocations on both benchmarks.

\section{Related Work}

Our work sits at the intersection of classical RAG, agentic retrieval, and educational tutoring benchmarks. Classical RAG fixes a retrieve-then-read controller~\cite{lewis2020rag}, while later systems relax that controller through learned or heuristic retrieval decisions such as Self-RAG, CRAG, Adaptive-RAG, FRAG, LLM-Independent Adaptive RAG, RetrievalQA, and TARG~\cite{asai2024selfrag,yan2024crag,jeong2024adaptiverag,gao2025frag,marina2025llmindependentrag,zhang2024retrievalqa,wang2026targ}. Our claim is narrower than novelty over selective retrieval in general: we test a tutoring-specific route-and-context design in which raw tutoring samples are routed first and the hybrid pipeline then assembles pedagogical context from planning, diagnosis, and rubric signals before optional retrieval. Evaluation and long-context studies further show that strong retrieval alone does not guarantee grounded generation~\cite{chen2023benchmarkingrag,ru2024ragchecker,friel2024ragbench,rau2024bergen,bai2024longbench2,liu2024lostmiddle}.

Agentic systems instead treat retrieval as one tool inside a larger coordination loop. ReAct, AutoGen, CAMEL, MetaGPT, MA-RAG, and related studies show that role decomposition can improve planning-heavy or staged reasoning tasks~\cite{yao2023react,wu2024autogen,li2023camel,hong2024metagpt,nguyen2025marag,radeva2025coordination,liu2023agentbench}. Our paper makes that comparison explicit for tutoring by holding the downstream tutor fixed and varying only how tutoring context is assembled before generation.

Education-focused evaluation has also shifted from short-form answer accuracy toward pedagogy, adaptivity, and instructional safety. TutorEval measures chapter-grounded science tutoring with expected teaching key points~\cite{chevalier2024sciencetutors}. EduBench is broader and more heterogeneous, spanning grading, feedback, emotional support, and personalized-learning scenarios~\cite{xu2025edubench}. In our released evaluator, TutorEval rows are scored through key-point proxies, while EduBench rows are scored through structured score/detail/feedback outputs and 12 rubric-style dimensions. Related tutoring benchmarks further reinforce the same methodological point: when evaluation measures rubric adherence and calibrated help, a system's \emph{grounding mechanism} cannot be judged on retrieval recall alone~\cite{macina2025mathtutorbench,srinivasa2025tutorbench,hazra2026safetutors}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Our proposed framework, \textsc{Multi-Agent Retrieval}, is a training-free tutoring pipeline that combines route-first control with context-first tutoring. We represent each tutoring sample as a bundle of student request, optional dialogue history, optional chapter/context text, rubric information, and benchmark metadata. The released app first computes a route from those raw fields. In the hybrid pipeline, that precomputed route is then used while planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules build a short tutoring brief: what the student is asking, what personalized learning state is visible, what a good answer should satisfy, and whether outside evidence is still needed. The key idea is to move answer generation away from retrieve-first control while keeping the downstream tutor and critic fixed. Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} sketches this pipeline.

This separation matters because the two benchmarks stress different failure modes. In TutorEval, the missing information is often chapter-grounded but pedagogically scoped: the tutor must connect the question to the right example, equation, or misconception inside a long chapter. In EduBench, the missing information is often student-specific: the system must react to profile fields, grading criteria, or scenario constraints that are already present in the sample but are not naturally retrievable from a topical query. A retrieve-first controller treats these cases too similarly. Our pipeline makes the tutoring brief explicit before answer generation, even though the top-level route is computed first.

To isolate that design choice, all implemented mechanisms share the same tutor/critic backbone, corpus, reranker, context packer, and response budget; the only designed difference is \emph{how supporting context is assembled before the tutor writes}. For each benchmark we freeze one common retrieval index and keep its construction unchanged across mechanisms. We omit one additional agentic-retrieval variant from the main table because it does not have a fully matched slice on every benchmark; including it would reintroduce unequal-example-set comparisons. The design principle is simple: retrieve when external evidence is genuinely needed, and otherwise spend the budget on building a better pedagogical view of the learner and the grading criteria. Backbone calls are therefore treated as an observed efficiency outcome rather than a quantity forced to be equal by design. The head-to-head tables should thus be read as controlled pipeline ablations under shared routing metadata, not as a benchmark of one online router making a four-way architecture choice.

\subsection{Regime-aware routing}

The released router is evaluated before any tutoring pipeline runs. It supports three decision sources: a trained bag-of-ngrams classifier when one is loaded, dataset-family priors otherwise, and the cue-based fallback described below. For each sample $x$, the fallback constructs a normalized text blob $b(x)$ from the question, optional context, rubric text, and dialogue history, and computes cue-match rates
\begin{equation}
\kappa_G(x)=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{u\in G}\mathbb{1}[u \subseteq b(x)],
\end{equation}
for five cue sets: evidence ($E$), coordination ($C$), rubric ($R$), planning ($P$), and tutoring ($T$). Each score follows one common form,
\begin{equation}
s_j(x)=\operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\kappa_j(x)+b_j(x)+\pi_j(d_x)\right),
\end{equation}
for $j\in\{e,c,r,p,t\}$, where $b_j(x)$ is a small sample-dependent bonus and $\pi_j(d_x)$ is the dataset prior. In the released configuration, $b_e=0.08\,\mathbb{1}[\text{context}]+0.05\,\mathbb{1}[\text{image}]$, $b_c=0.03\,\mathbb{1}[\text{image}]$, $b_r=0.20\,\mathbb{1}[\text{rubric}]$, $b_p=0$, and $b_t=\min(|h_x|/6,0.5)$. Dataset priors add $+0.15$ to evidence for evidence-grounded datasets, $+0.15$ to coordination and rubric for rubric-feedback datasets, $+0.15$ to coordination and tutoring for adaptive-tutoring datasets, and $(+0.12,+0.18)$ to coordination and planning for lesson-planning datasets. High $s_e$ means the sample looks evidence-grounded; high $s_c$, $s_r$, $s_p$, or $s_t$ indicate increasingly coordination-heavy tutoring, rubric, planning, or dialogue-sensitive cases.

Architecture selection is then a fixed case split:
\begin{equation}
A(x)=
\begin{cases}
\text{RAG} & s_e\ge 0.52,\; s_c<0.35,\\
\text{A-RAG} & s_e\ge 0.35,\; s_c\ge 0.50,\\
\text{MA} & s_c\ge 0.55,\; s_e<0.28,\\
\text{Hybrid} & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
This case split describes the fallback router's family choices. High evidence with low coordination maps to classical retrieve-then-read, high evidence with high coordination maps to agentic RAG, and high coordination with little evidence maps to multi-agent tutoring without retrieval. Borderline cases fall into the hybrid family. The paper's \textsc{Single-Agent (no retrieval)} baseline is a separate ablation floor and is not produced by the router. The pedagogical regime is resolved separately. If the dataset adapter provides a regime hint, the router uses it directly; otherwise it sets
\begin{equation}
R(x)=
\begin{cases}
\text{LP} & s_p\ge \max\{s_r,s_t,0.42\},\\
\text{RF} & s_r\ge \max\{s_t,0.35\},\\
\text{AT} & s_t\ge 0.35,\\
\text{EG} & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where LP, RF, AT, and EG denote lesson planning, rubric feedback, adaptive tutoring, and evidence-grounded reasoning. Within the proposed hybrid family, retrieval is gated by
\begin{equation}
g(x)=\mathbb{1}[s_e(x)\ge 0.35 \;\lor\; R(x)=\text{EG}],
\end{equation}
which matches the production threshold in the codebase. Inside the hybrid pipeline, if $g(x)=0$, the system still builds the tutoring brief and skips retrieval unless a later fallback is triggered. If the first draft still has no retrieved grounding and $s_e(x)\ge 0.45$, the pipeline issues one fallback retrieval pass using the raw question. This fallback is important on mixed cases where the initial coordination view looks sufficient but the final answer still benefits from chapter or reference evidence.

\subsection{Specialist agents and shared context}

All implemented mechanisms operate over the same shared state: the tutoring prompt, the inferred personalized learning state, the rubric summary, any retrieved evidence, and the evolving draft answer. \textbf{The tutor and the critic are held constant across all implemented mechanisms}: the tutor writes from the supplied context, and the critic revises only when it identifies a concrete issue. The \emph{only} moving parts are the upstream modules that prepare retrieval context. In the released paper configuration, the planner, diagnoser, and rubric agent are lightweight deterministic heuristics rather than separate LLM workers. The planner emits up to three deduplicated search queries formed from the raw question, extracted key terms, dataset cueing, and rubric terms. The diagnoser summarizes personalized learning state as level, goals, preferred style, and likely misconceptions from prompt and dialogue cues. The rubric agent turns explicit rubric items into a short checklist or falls back to a default tutoring rubric.

The tutor itself is dataset-aware but architecturally fixed. Its prompt includes the dataset name, question, recent dialogue, inferred personalized learning state, operating plan, rubric summary, and either retrieved evidence or inline benchmark context. It is always instructed to be correct, pedagogically useful, concise, and to end with a clear next step or check-for-understanding when appropriate. When retrieved evidence is present, the tutor must cite it with \texttt{[doc\_id]} markers. For EduBench consensus rows, the tutor is further constrained to emit a single JSON object containing a score, scoring details, and personalized feedback, because that benchmark evaluates structured educational judgment. For TutorEval and the other tutoring-style profiles, the tutor emits ordinary instructional prose. This design isolates the effect of changing the retrieval context while leaving the downstream writer unchanged.

\subsection{Hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context packing}

The retrieval stack is intentionally lightweight and also follows the codebase exactly. For a query $q$ and chunk $c$, the hybrid index computes
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{score}_0
&=0.58\,k_w+0.17\,k_{ch}\\
&\quad+0.25\,k_{\ell}+\beta,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where all terms are computed for the current $(q,c)$ pair. Here $k_w$ is word-level TF--IDF similarity, $k_{ch}$ is character $3$--$5$ gram TF--IDF similarity, and $k_{\ell}$ is the similarity in the truncated-SVD latent space. The query boost $\beta$ adds $0.01$ per overlapping title token and, in citation-seeking mode, an additional $0.025$ for chunks whose source type is \emph{reference}, \emph{lecture}, or \emph{document}. When multiple planner queries are issued, the retriever merges all returned candidates and deduplicates them by chunk identifier before reranking.

In the released paper configuration, no trained reranker is loaded, so the heuristic reranker score is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{score}_1
&=0.55\,\mathrm{score}_0+0.20\,o_t+0.10\,o_h\\
&\quad+0.06\,m_p+0.05\,o_n+0.04\,\rho,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where all terms are again computed for the current $(q,c)$ pair; $o_t$, $o_h$, $m_p$, and $o_n$ denote token overlap, title overlap, exact phrase match, and numeric overlap, and $\rho$ is a brevity prior. Final context packing uses maximum marginal relevance:
\begin{equation}
c^\star=\arg\max_{c\in \mathcal{C}\setminus S}
\lambda\,\mathrm{score}_1(q,c)-(1-\lambda)\max_{c'\in S}\mathrm{overlap}(c,c'),
\end{equation}
with $\lambda=0.68$, a four-chunk cap, and a $6000$-character context budget. This keeps the comparison focused on retrieval quality rather than on simply enlarging the context window.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}

Evaluation is dataset-aware, but the reported metrics are internal automatic proxies implemented in the released evaluator rather than official benchmark scoring scripts. The headline table's composite score (\textit{Comp.}) is a paper-level weighted summary from the released analysis pipeline, reported only as a convenience overview; we interpret the task-specific metrics directly. Token-F1 is the token-overlap F1 between the model answer and the benchmark reference answer. Rubric coverage is the fraction of rubric items the answer covers, using either near-verbatim phrase match or sufficiently strong token overlap anchored by a content-bearing token. Latency, total tokens, and backbone-call count capture inference cost, and backbone calls correspond to the released system's \texttt{llm\_call\_count}.

For EduBench, we additionally report EB12D, the mean over 12 rubric-oriented signals spanning scenario adaptation, factual reasoning, and pedagogical application. Those signals reward output-format compliance, supportive but non-negative tone, relevance to the question, use of scenario elements from the sample, alignment with the benchmark score, domain-knowledge grounding, reasoning quality, error identification, clarity, motivational guidance, personalization, and higher-order prompting. We report EduAlign as $1-|s(y)-\bar{s}|/100$, where $s(y)$ is the score extracted from the model's JSON output and $\bar{s}$ is the benchmark reference score.

For TutorEval, we report tutor-hit as the mean key-point credit over the $K$ expected teaching points, with full credit for explicit coverage, partial credit for moderate token overlap, and zero otherwise. We also track a chapter-grounding proxy by measuring whether the response stays aligned with retrieved evidence when retrieval is used, or with the inline chapter text otherwise. Concretely, EduBench is evaluated as a structured educational-judgment task, whereas TutorEval is evaluated as a chapter-grounded tutoring-response task. In the per-example evaluator outputs, off-profile metrics remain present as zeros; in the paper tables, benchmark-inapplicable columns are simply not shown. The released code also records a workload audit $U=T+160Q+90C+240A+30E$, where $T$ is total tokens, $Q$ retrieval queries, $C$ retrieved chunks, $A$ materialized agent outputs, and $E$ trace events. This score is used only as an efficiency audit, not as a training objective. A retrieval-agnostic corpus-factuality check is supported when a compatible shared index is available, but the archived main comparison predates that audit, so we do not present it as a headline result here.

\label{sec:results}

We run frozen head-to-head comparisons under matched 4B infrastructure (\textsc{Qwen3.5-4B}, reasoning budget 512, temperature 0.15, response cap 900). TutorEval uses all $834$ matched samples and EduBench uses the $1562$-sample four-way intersection; retrieval hyperparameters are fixed at the \S\ref{sec:methodology} values.

\subsection{Primary comparison: TutorEval}

Table~\ref{tab:updated_quality_summary} reports paired matched-sample comparisons with bootstrap confidence intervals and permutation p-values. On TutorEval, changing the retrieval context raises Token-F1 by $+0.004$, tutor-hit by $+0.019$, and rubric coverage by $+0.026$, while using 484 fewer tokens; latency trends lower but is not reliable ($p{=}0.21$). Because retrieval is active on every TutorEval sample, this benchmark tests whether coordination still helps when chapter evidence is always consulted. Retrieval-free multi-agent and single-agent baselines lag, showing that the fixed tutor does not recover chapter-specific key points reliably without corpus support.

\subsection{EduBench intersection and the capacity confound}

On the EduBench intersection, multi-agent retrieval improves EB12D by $+0.031$ and rubric coverage by $+0.186$, while reducing latency by $5{,}923$\,ms, tokens by $1{,}794$, and backbone calls by 0.49. Classical RAG keeps a small EduAlign edge, but retrieval is skipped on every EduBench sample in the frozen run, indicating that these prompts are usually better served by learner- and rubric-centered coordination than by external lookup. The hybrid therefore behaves almost identically to retrieval-free multi-agent coordination on EduBench ($\Delta_{\text{EB12D}}=+0.001$, $p{=}0.61$) while clearly outperforming classical RAG.

\subsection{Ablations: attributing the hybrid's gains}
\label{sec:ablations}

Two executed 4B ablations on a shared TutorEval slice ($n{=}100$) isolate the mechanism. Forcing retrieval hurts Token-F1 ($-0.003$), tutor-hit ($-0.015$), and rubric coverage ($-0.025$) with little token change ($-36$), so the gate is functionally active. Turning the shared critic off improves all scalar metrics and cuts tokens by $27\%$ ($-891$), implying that the hybrid gain does not come from the critic. On a pinned \textsc{Qwen3.5-27B-FP8} backbone, all eight sessions completed: TutorEval score deltas collapse near zero, but hybrid remains more efficient ($-992$ tokens, $-11{,}845$\,ms), and the EduBench pattern repeats on the 27B intersection ($n{=}87$) with EB12D $+0.032$, rubric $+0.210$, tokens $-1{,}861$, and latency $-22{,}070$\,ms.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The evidence supports a narrower claim than ``agents are better tutors'': multi-agent coordination can replace the retriever as the main context-construction mechanism. Planner, diagnoser, and rubric modules perform work that classical RAG delegates to one embedded query, and the quality gains do not coincide with extra backbone use: tutor/critic calls fall from 1.84 to 1.56 on TutorEval and from 1.97 to 1.48 on EduBench. The remaining caveat is structural rather than computational, because hybrid adds deterministic coordination modules even when backbone usage drops.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Limitations remain. The study fixes backbone, cap, corpus, and retriever but not the full system structure; the archived main comparison predates the retrieval-agnostic corpus-factuality audit and uses automatic rather than human evaluation; and the scope is limited to two open-weight backbones, one shared corpus per benchmark, English-only data, and benchmark outputs rather than downstream learning gains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Under shared infrastructure, multi-agent retrieval improves fixed-tutor quality and efficiency on TutorEval and especially on the EduBench intersection. In educational tutoring, better context assembly before generation can beat classical retrieve-first control.
