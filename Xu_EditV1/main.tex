\begin{abstract}
This study examines when multi-agent large language model systems should replace or extend classical retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) in educational settings. The argument is intentionally narrow: multi-agent systems are not presented as a universal substitute for retrieval, but as a potentially stronger orchestration pattern for educational tasks whose main difficulty lies in coordination rather than document access alone. The paper is framed as a taxonomy and evaluation-protocol blueprint rather than as a completed empirical study. It organizes educational tasks along two axes---knowledge grounding need and pedagogical coordination complexity---and uses that framing to compare classical RAG, agentic RAG, and non-RAG multi-agent systems. The proposed evaluation protocol emphasizes groundedness, pedagogical quality, adaptivity, robustness, cost, and latency under shared budgets and shared task conditions, while the benchmark mapping distinguishes education-facing evidence from transferable mechanism-level stress tests.
\end{abstract}
